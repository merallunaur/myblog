The Embalmer of the Bastille 巴士底的入殮師

They demanded that Arthur meet with the forensic medicine students they had assigned to him, and hastily informed him that this was the only way he would stay alive. They feared this British physician who had come from afar—he was intelligent, cold, his expression always too sullen, like a London winter morning rain. In truth, none of these power-wielders had ever been to London; some of them had never even stepped outside their own farmhouses. But they knew they urgently needed new learning—a sanitised history that could be written thoughtlessly into the annals, proof of the progress of civilisation. He had served among the people for six years. He was twenty-six now. The students he was to meet were seven or eight years younger, fiddling with the wide-mouthed bottles filled with solution on the table. 他們要求亞瑟與他們指派的法醫學生們見面，並懷著倉促敬告他這是他活命的唯一方式。他們畏懼這個遠道而來的英國醫師——他聰明、冰冷、表情總是太過陰鷙，仿若倫敦冬日早晨的一場雨。事實上他們這些掌權者誰也沒有到過倫敦，他們中的一些甚至從未走出過自家的農舍。但他們知道自己亟需新的學術，能不假思索地寫入史冊中的美化過的歷史，文明進步的證明。他曾在民間服務六年。他今年二十六歲，會面的學生比他年紀小上七八歲，正擺弄著檯子上裝滿溶液的廣口瓶。
The first corpse delivered had died not long ago, its hands folded across its sunken chest. He examined the body as usual: multiple rib fractures, internal bleeding, deformed feet—it had fallen from a great height. Time of death was less than four hours ago, meaning it had been rushed here almost as soon as it stopped struggling. Its coffin lay to one side—a grey-black sack printed with a potato pattern. 第一具送來的屍體剛死不久，雙手交疊著放在乾癟的胸口。他像往常一樣檢查了那具屍體：肋骨多處斷裂，內臟出血，雙足變形——他從很高的地方跳下來。死亡時間不超過四個小時，意味著他剛一停止掙扎就被急吼吼地運到了這裡。他的棺槨停放在一邊，一條灰黑色麻袋，上面印著土豆圖樣。
"Internal chest bleeding, rib fractures..." 「胸內出血、肋骨斷裂……」
He repeated to his students the medical terms that were obscure to them. This was not the medicine they craved, but he did not mind. Such unnatural deaths would become frequent, and in time everyone would grow accustomed. 他向著他的學生們重複著對於他們來說晦澀難懂的醫學名詞。這不應該是他們所渴求的醫學，但他並不介意，這類非自然死亡以後將會時有發生，最後每個人都將習以為常。
"Is this the first corpse you have dissected?" 「這是你解剖的第一具屍體嗎？」
Someone among the students asked. 學生當中有人問道。
He shook his head and looked up at them. He could detect the other's rural accent; the boy did not understand the meaning of forensic medicine, perhaps could not even understand his speech. He scrutinised the student's innocent face with a harsh gaze—smooth, unlined eyelids, full cheeks, radiating a naive childishness. Youth had granted him great favour. 他搖頭，抬頭看向他們。他能聽出對方的鄉下口音，對方並不知道法醫課程的意義，甚至可能聽不懂他講話。他用刻薄的眼光審視著學生單純的臉——眼尾平整而光滑，雙頰飽滿，透露出一種無知的稚氣。年輕給予了他極大的恩惠。
"He struggled for at least two hours." 「他至少掙扎了兩個小時。」
He frowned. Ordinarily, a fall victim would die instantly; the less fortunate might linger for a few more minutes. He lowered the corpse's hands, which had been folded over its chest, now stiff as plaster. A small copper crucifix hung from its neck, falling out from between its clasped palms. He removed the cross and carefully placed it in the corpse's breast pocket. Faith—the death of faith, the erosion of faith—silent and stealthy. Thus did they hope for the afterlife. 他皺著眉頭。一般情況下墜亡者會當場斃命，不太走運的會再睏幾分鐘。他放下屍體在胸前合十的手，像石膏像般發硬。屍體脖子上掛著一個小小的銅質十字架，從合十的掌縫間掉落出來。他把十字架取下來，小心翼翼地放進屍體的上衣口袋。信仰，信仰的死亡，信仰的流失，悄無聲息。如此希冀往生。
He lowered his head and demonstrated how to proceed. The corpse's hanging fingers were immersed in the solution, to facilitate examination of cuts and abrasions. The second corpse brought in had died from stab wounds; he knew such wounds intimately. The prison produced a great many such bodies every day. He had once been ordered to climb atop a pile of corpses and swear allegiance to the state. After a cold lecture, he handed the body over to his students, stepped aside, and lifted the sack cloth from the third corpse on his own. 他低下頭，向他們演示如何操作。屍體垂下的手指被浸泡進溶液，以便檢查割傷和擦傷。送進來的第二具屍體死於刺傷，他對這類傷口瞭如指掌。監獄裡每天會產生大量的這種屍體，他曾經被要求爬到屍體堆上去，向國家宣誓。一番冷冰冰的說教後，他把屍體交給他的學生，走到一邊，逕自掀開第三具屍體的麻布。
Golden hair, hanging down from the edge of the rubber bed, like lifeless withered grass. A brief vertigo; he leaned against the wall. His vanished lover—after two months, he had finally reunited with him. 金色長髮，從橡膠床的邊緣垂下來，像了無生氣的枯草。一瞬間的短暫眩暈，他扶住了牆壁。他消失的愛人，時隔兩個月，他終於與他重逢。
He dismissed the students, telling them he needed rest, and then sat down beside the body. He saw the tattoo on the corpse's ankle—freshly inked, still crusted with black blood scabs, like small stones driven into the skin. One thousand seven hundred ninety-two—the number they had given him, a mobile digit. He rested his head on the edge of the rubber table and reached his hand into the sack cloth. He did not check the condition of his pupils, but inferred that he had been killed about fifteen hours earlier. Fifteen hours ago—he could not recall what he had been doing then. Living normally felt like a sin. He reached out, took the corpse's wrist, and pressed his thumb heavily against its pulse. 他遣散了學生，告訴他們他需要休息，旋即在屍體身邊坐下。他看見他腳踝上刺著刺青——剛刻上去，還結著漆黑的血痂，像腳踝刺進去了幾粒小石子。一千七百九十二，他們給他的編號，一個移動的數字。他把頭擱在橡膠檯子上，手伸進麻布裡。他沒有檢查他的瞳孔狀況，但可以推斷他大概在十五小時前遇害。十五小時前，他想不起那時自己正在幹什麼——正常生活彷彿成為一種罪過。他伸過手握住他的手腕，大拇指重重壓在他的脈搏上。
"The Republic is not born virtuous." 「共和國並非生來美德。」
He remembered his lover saying these words. They had been sitting on the floor of the attic, three months earlier, when he was helping him remove a piece of shrapnel from his thigh. His lover had held his shoulders, transferring his weight and part of his pain onto him. Their boots were drying on the windowsill, freshly scrubbed, likely stained with someone else's blood. The Republic is not born virtuous; when the Republic decides what virtue is, some become its sacrifice. 他想起他愛人說過的這句話。那時他們坐在閣樓的地板上，三個月前，他正幫他取出大腿裡的一枚彈片。他的愛人抱住他的肩膀，將重量與一部分疼痛轉移到他身上。他們的靴子晾在窗台上，鞋底剛剛刷過，上面很可能沾著另一個人的血。共和國並非生來美德，當共和國決定美德時，有人便成了共和國的祭品。
He could recall nothing—neither their meeting, nor their knowing, nor their parting. Everything surged forward and receded backward, sinking into his solitude, finally turning into a haze of smoke. He pressed his ear against the corpse's chest, like a child pressing a seashell to his ear. He heard the sound of waves, heard poetry churning in the silver-white surf. After a while, he gripped the edge of the table, slowly stood, opened the cabinet, and took out cypress branches to burn as incense. There had always been such a bundle in his cabinet, ever since two months ago. He placed the dying cypress branches beside the corpse's bed, opened his arms, and embraced it, pressing its cold body as close to his own as possible, winding his ten fingers through its thin, slender fingers. He remembered his lover standing atop a moving carriage, thrusting a flag straight into the sky, his ten fingers tightly gripping the pole. He stood upright, his shoulders draped in the sun's halo, the skin around his neck marked with abrasions from a hemp rope. His lover had been twenty-four then—young, vigorous, wild, as every fervent life is. 他回憶不起任何事情——相遇、相知以及別離。一切都向前翻湧又向後退去，沉浸入他的孤獨，最後化為一片煙霧。他把耳朵貼在屍體的胸口，彷彿孩子把耳朵貼緊海螺。他聽見海浪聲，聽見銀白色的浪花中翻騰的詩歌。過了一會兒，他撐著床沿，緩緩站起身，打開立櫃，取出柏樹枝為他焚香。他的櫃子裡一向都有這麼一束，由兩個月之前開始。他把將要燃盡的柏樹枝放在他的床邊，張開雙臂環抱著他，把他冰涼的軀體盡可能貼近自己的身軀，十指纏繞著他細瘦的十指。他記得他的愛人站在行駛的馬車頂端，將手中的旗幟直插向天空，十指緊緊握著旗桿。筆直地站著，肩膀上披著日暉，脖子周圍有被麻繩刮擦過的血跡。那時他愛人二十四歲，年輕、活力而野性，就像每個熱烈的生命。
Their love had been deeply secret. His lover had twice tried to kiss him—on the dew-covered balcony, their faces smeared with tricolour paint. A storm approached them from the north; they sat beneath an oak tree, a cool breeze blowing, with no serious conversation. His lover suddenly leaned in, smeared paint on his cheeks, then stood and tried to wipe his face with a damp handkerchief. And the country lanes they used to wander together, in the gentle breeze of the open fields mixed with a medicinal fragrance of flowers, his lover had described to him with great delight the rural life he imagined—lounge chairs, horseback riding, the soothing light rain of the evening, what flowers to plant in which months. And that dilapidated stone-brick house, which he said would one day become his home. Under the pale blue moonlight, he had stepped on wet moss, struggling to climb over the wall, waved to him, then leaped down. He heard the soft sound of his landing. After a moment, he appeared from behind the worm-eaten black wooden door, holding a few flowers in his hand—perhaps prickly lettuces, which had cut his fingers, the blood melting into the frost-covered soil of the evening. In the distance came the tranquil evening bells of the church; the pilgrim's road seemed close at hand. 他們的感情相當隱密。他的愛人曾兩度試圖與他接吻——在沾滿露水的陽台上，滿臉塗著三色油彩。暴風雨從北面朝他們襲來，他們坐在橡樹下，涼風習習，沒有多少嚴肅的交談。他的愛人突然靠近，將他的兩頰沾上油彩，隨即站起身，用打濕的手帕試圖擦乾面頰。以及他們曾經閒逛去的鄉間小路，在曠野的微風和一陣帶有藥味的花香中，他向他興高采烈地描述他所設想的鄉下生活——躺椅、騎馬、夜間怡情的小雨、包括幾月該種什麼花。以及那幢破敗的石磚房子，他說將來會成為他的家。在淡藍色月光的映照下，他踩著濕漉的青苔奮力攀上圍牆，向他招手後縱身跳下，他聽見他落地的輕響。過了一會兒他從蟲蛀的黑色木門後出現，手裡握著幾朵花，可能是刺苞菊，劃傷了他的手指，血融進晚間結著白霜的土壤中。遠處傳來教堂寧靜的晚鐘，朝聖之路彷彿近在咫尺。
Two months ago, his lover had failed to keep an appointment. He had either disappeared, or was already dead. 兩個月前，他的愛人未能赴一個約會，他要嘛失蹤了，要嘛已經死了。
He heard the sound of mice gnawing at the wallpaper. He smoothed his lover's long hair, which was slowly losing its lustre like old brocade. Then he buttoned his buttons, wrapped his abdomen in bandages to cover the ugly gunshot wound, and fastened each button from the first to the last. When he had finished, he took the corpse's hand again, as if the stagnant blood beneath that hand were seeping little by little into his own skin. Everything dead, mistaken, unresolved—all of it wandered through his body. He had long anticipated this situation. He had known it would happen. After stupidity had delivered its verdict, he might kill him once more, display him indifferently under the light of day, for everyone to speculate about his life. 他聽見老鼠啃食牆紙的聲音，便將愛人的長髮整理好，它們正像舊錦緞一樣緩緩失去光澤。接著他扣好他的扣子，將腹部纏繞上繃帶，遮住那個醜陋的槍傷；把他的扣子從第一顆扣到最後一顆。做完這一切後，他再次握住他的手，彷彿那隻手下停滯的血液正一點一點滲進他的皮膚。死掉的、錯誤的、懸而未決的一切都在他身體裡遊走。他早已對這種狀況有心理預想。他早就料到了，當愚鈍寫下判決之後，他可能會再一次殺死他，把他漠然地擺在白日之下，供每個人去猜測他的一生。
Tomorrow would come again. He raised his head weakly toward the window. Dark clouds had completely obscured the moon; the tree shadows filtering through the window tumbled down from the wall. A thick, impenetrable darkness. The city seemed never to have opened its eyes. 明日又將前來。他無力地抬頭望向窗外，烏雲把月亮遮得嚴嚴實實，窗戶透進來的樹影從牆上滾落。一片濃得化不開的暗夜。城市彷彿從未睜開過眼睛。
