Through the Tearful Eyes of a Butterfly 拂過蝴蝶的淚眼

My esteemed teacher, Arthur Kirkland, passed away last night. When the call came, I was fast asleep in a small cottage at the foot of Ben Nevis, where I had been studying a rare species of beetle. My teacher was a distinguished entomologist. 我受人尊敬的老师，亞瑟·柯克蘭，昨天夜裡去世了。接到電話時，我正在本尼維斯山腳下的一個小屋裡酣睡，我在那裡研究一種罕見的甲蟲，我的老師是一位卓越的昆蟲學家。
The next morning, I drove to the railway station in Fort William. The road passed through the vast Scottish highlands, with silent mountains standing in the distance. I watched the bright white stars gradually fade in the golden-red light of the sun, finally sinking into the dark green mountains, tracing a gleaming silver arc across the lingering deep blue canopy of the sky. 第二天清晨，我驅車前往威廉堡的火車站。這一段公路從蘇格蘭的廣袤原野上經過，遠處矗立著沉默的群山。我看見一顆顆白亮的星星漸漸在太陽的金紅色光芒中變得黯淡，最終沉入青黑色的群山之間，在那殘留的深藍天幕上劃開一道閃亮的銀弧。
There were not many people at the funeral. My teacher had never married, and his relatives had long since drifted away. Most of those in attendance were his students, like myself. His adopted daughter, Olivia, paced back and forth, her pale cheeks streaked with tears—one trail had barely vanished before another crept over the same path. She wore a black dress and, upon seeing me, walked over. 葬禮人不是很多，我的老師一生未婚，親戚也早已疏遠，葬禮上大多都是像我一樣的學生。他的養女，奧莉薇婭，不斷走來走去，青白色的雙頰上滿是淚痕，一條還沒消失，就有一條新的淚痕又爬在原來的那條上。她穿著一件黑色的禮服，看到我就向我走來。
"Please help me," she said softly, sighing, looking at me with pleading eyes. "I really don't know what to do. He often said you were his most brilliant student... Well, if you're willing, please come with me." She led me upstairs, into her father's bedroom. The furnishings were extremely simple: an oak desk, a small wardrobe, a bed. Nailed to the headboard was a brass hook, from which hung an old specimen case. The pinewood grain on the wooden frame had grown deeper with the erosion of time, like wrinkles spreading across a once-smooth face. In the centre was a false butterfly inlaid with gold thread and enamel. The bed had not been made since that night; the quilt lay in a heap, as if someone still lived there. 「請幫幫我。」她輕飄飄地說，嘆了口氣，用求助的眼光看著我，「我實在不知道怎麼辦了，他常說您是他最得意的學生……唉…如果您願意的話，請跟我來。」她引我上樓，走入她父親的臥室。屋裡陳設極其簡單，一張橡木書桌，一個小型衣櫃，一張床。床頭釘了個銅釘，上面掛著一個老舊的標本匣，木質邊框上的松木紋路，隨著時間的磨蝕而愈加深刻，如同皺紋爬滿了一張光潔的臉。中央是一只用金絲和琺瑯鑲嵌成的假蝴蝶。床鋪從那天晚上就沒整理過，被子胡亂堆在床上，讓人覺得這裡彷彿還住著人。
Olivia knelt by the bed, bent down to look underneath, and pulled out an old, large suitcase. She opened it and handed me a thick notebook. 奧莉薇婭在床邊跪下，彎腰看向床底，伸手拖出一個陳舊的大行李箱，打開了，掏出一個厚筆記本遞給我。
"I hesitated for a long time about whether to let anyone know about this," she said. "But... to be honest, I know very little about him. So I thought, perhaps I should find someone to discuss it with." I opened the notebook. Tiny, dense handwriting leaped into my eyes—my teacher's script. As I read, it was as if each page were a reel of film, and when spliced together and threaded through a projector, they would show a story: 「我猶豫了很久要不要讓人知道。」她說，「但……坦白說，我對他知之甚少，所以我想著，也許我該找個人商量。」我翻開筆記本，密密麻麻的小字跳入眼中，是我老師的字體。我讀著，彷彿每一頁紙都是一管膠卷，連在一起，插入放映機裡，把一段故事給人看：

I am seeing less and less. Even so, the images in my dreams grow clearer by the day. When one is young, one dreams of the future—of drinking coffee in a place one has longed to visit, of kissing one's lover under the aurora. But as one ages, one dreams more and more frequently of things long past. I have decided to record this past—perhaps it is my dream. At the very least, I will not let it gather dust in my increasingly dull mind. 我越來越看不見東西了。即便如此，我每日夢中的景象卻越來越清晰。年輕時，人會夢到未來，夢到自己在嚮往了很久的地方喝咖啡，夢到與戀人在極光下接吻。年老時卻只能越來越頻繁地夢到以前的事情了。我決定記錄下這段往事——也許是我的夢境。至少，不讓它塵封於我日漸愚鈍的大腦。
I was born in Liverpool, a city I like to call 'impatient and reckless.' It is like a plump woman carrying a wicker basket, dashing through the market streets to snap up cheap cabbages. She runs so fast that, before she has enjoyed her youth, she hurries headlong into a self-righteous middle age. She cuts down trees and turns them into gold; she smokes the chimneys of steamships like cigars. Until I was sixteen, our entire family huddled together in a bridge arch, where there was almost no light. When I was sixteen, my father died from a workplace accident, and my mother received a compensation payment. To escape the grief of losing her husband and to find fresh air, she used the money to move us to a small town in Arrochar. 我出生於利物浦，我喜歡稱它是一座「急躁而冒失的城市」。它彷彿一個挎著柳條籃子，急急地跑在集市街道上想去搶購廉價甘藍的胖婦人。它跑得是那樣的快，以至於還未享受過青春年華就匆匆轉入自以為是的中年。它把樹砍下，變作金子；把汽船的煙囪當作雪茄來抽。在我十六歲以前，我們全家都蝸居在一個橋洞裡，那裡幾乎找不到光亮。十六歲時我的父親出工傷去世了，我的母親得到了一筆撫恤金。為了遠離失去丈夫的悲痛，也為了獲得新鮮空氣，她利用這筆錢設法使我們搬去了阿羅赫爾的一個小鎮。
There is no need to say much about this town. It nestles against a low, earthen hill covered in grass, with wildflowers scattered among the stems and leaves. In spring and summer, butterflies fluttered everywhere. Sometimes I would lie in the grass all day and fall asleep there, until the midnight dew soaked my clothes. When I opened my eyes, I would have the illusion that the countless stars were falling toward me. 關於這個小鎮無需贅述。它背靠著一座矮矮的土包，山上長滿青草，莖葉之間散落著野花，春夏時間蝴蝶紛飛。我有時會在草地上躺上一整天，然後就這麼睡著了，直到半夜的露水把我的衣裳打濕，我睜開眼，錯覺中滿天的星星都在向我墜落。
My mother cleared a vegetable patch on the flat land at the foot of the hill and occasionally washed clothes for others in the town. I delivered fresh eggs and milk from door to door. The residents here were polite enough to us, but there was always a cold distance between us. They rarely interacted with us and essentially regarded us as outsiders. 我的母親在山下的平地開闢了一片菜地，偶爾為鎮子裡的其他人家洗洗衣服，我則挨家挨戶地送新鮮雞蛋和牛奶。這裡的居民對我們還算客氣，但我與他們之間總有一種冷漠的疏離。他們仍與我們交集甚少，並在本質上把我們視作異鄉人。
Across from our house stood a tall, dilapidated tower of stone bricks. Unlike the other farmhouses with their square walls and sloping roofs, this one was a perfect cylinder, topped with a faded blue roof, with only two small windows at the very top. It looked as if it had been dismantled from a medieval castle. The locals warned their children not to go near the tower, saying a leper lived there. Whether true or not, the rumour spread, and my mother forbade me from approaching it as well. Every night before I fell asleep, I would stare out my window at the monotonous, dark green light coming from the window across the way, imagining a beautiful woman living among the rose bushes inside. 我們家對面是一座高大破敗的塔，用石磚砌成。與其他方牆斜頂的農家小屋不同，它呈一個標準的圓柱形，有著褪了色的藍色屋頂，並只在最高處開了兩個很小的窗戶，很像是中世紀古堡上拆下來的。當地人教導自家的孩子不要靠近這座塔樓，據說裡面住著一個痲瘋病人。這種不知真假的話傳了出來，母親便也不讓我靠近。每天臨睡前，我透過我的窗子，呆望著對面那窗戶透出單調的，發著暗綠色的燈光，幻想裡面住著一位玫瑰花叢中的美人。

Days should have flowed by like this—a calm, rippleless stream. But such swiftly flowing streams, when they happen to strike a protruding rock, immediately leap up in a spray of joyful foam. One morning, my mother went to London and would not return for about two months. As usual, I delivered eggs to each house. As I placed the eggs on one family's porch, I heard an unmelodious whistle, and then someone called my name: 日子本該這麼流淌過去，像一條平靜而毫無波瀾的小溪。但是這些流得很快的小溪，總是稍微撞到一塊突出的岩石，就馬上翻出一簇喜悅的浪花來。某天早上，我的母親去倫敦了，約兩個月才能回來。我照常為每家遞送雞蛋。當我把雞蛋放在一家的門廊上時，我聽見一聲並不清脆的口哨聲，接著有人叫我的名字：
"Hey, gentleman!" A piece of popcorn was thrown at my feet. A tall, fat child leaned against a pitifully thin pillar, watching me. He stood in the shadow of the porch, revealing a row of teeth covered in corn husks. He had been harassing me for a long time, but I have since forgotten his name. That is entirely reasonable, for human memory is selfish and only favours what it loves. 「嘿，紳士！」一顆爆米花被扔在我的腳下，一個又高又胖的孩子倚在一根細得可憐的柱子上看我。他站在門廊的陰影中，露出一排沾著玉米皮的牙齒。他騷擾我很久了，但我如今已經忘了他的名字。這是完全合理的，因為人的記憶是自私的，只偏愛那些自己愛的東西。
In any case, he looked down at me: "Do you dare go into that broken-down house across from yours?" I said nothing, gave him a cold look, and prepared to leave with my bicycle. 總之，他俯視著我：「你敢不敢到你家對面的那個破房子裡走一趟？」我不說話，冷冷地看了他一眼，準備推著自行車離開。
"If you go in, I'll never bother you again!" he shouted after me, in a great hurry. 「你進去，我以後就再也不煩你了！」他急匆匆地在我身後大喊道。
To me, that was an extremely tempting condition. I turned my head. The child looked at me with a smug grin and said, "I'll be waiting for you at the door at six o'clock tonight." It was spring then, and darkness fell quickly. The thick, impenetrable night poured from the sky over the fields toward the horizon. The giant who ruled the day closed his golden eyes with sun for pupils and untied the rope that bound the sky. The deep blue curtain of heaven slowly descended. The boy stood about forty feet from the door, apparently afraid of the house. The building stood silently behind him, solemn and still, like a mourner at a funeral. Its tones almost merged with the surrounding night, becoming an inconspicuous frame in a black-and-white silent film. 對我來說，這是個極其誘人的條件。我轉過頭去，那孩子看著我，露出一副洋洋得意的笑臉，說：「今晚六點，我在門口等你。」那時是春天，天黑得很快，濃得化不開的黑夜由原野上空向地平線潑灑。掌管白天的巨人闔起他太陽做瞳仁的金色眸子，將繫在穹際繩子解開，深青色的天帷緩緩下落。那個男孩站在離門口約四十英尺的位置，似乎對這間房子有所畏懼。那座房子在他身後靜靜站著，莊嚴又沉靜，宛如一個參加葬禮的人。它的色調幾乎與四周的夜色融為一體，成為黑白默片中不起眼的一幀。
"Go in... bring me something, a piece of wood or whatever, to prove you've been there." The boy's carrot-like fingers clenched his clothes. His calves were as thick as an elephant's, and they were trembling violently. His eyes were slightly red, and he was drenched in sweat. After saying this, he ran off swiftly. I had never seen him run so fast. His flesh scattered and lifted as if trying to escape from his own body. 「進去吧……給我帶樣東西，一片木頭或是什麼其他的，證明你去了。」男孩如胡蘿蔔一樣的手指攥緊了他的衣服，他的小腿像小象的腿一般粗壯，此刻正抖個不停。他的眼眶微微發紅，汗流浹背。他對我說完這句話後就飛快地跑走了，我從未見他跑得這樣快，他全身的肥肉散開又飛起，彷彿要逃離他身上。
I walked up the steps and gently knocked on the rusty door knocker. No one answered, but the door seemed unlocked, so I pushed it open and went inside. The light switch was right by the door. I turned it on. 我走上台階，輕輕叩了叩生鏽的門環，無人應答，但門似乎沒上鎖，於是我推開門走了進去。燈的開關就在門邊，我打開了燈。
It was a strange feeling—like accidentally opening a dusty old cardboard box under the bed and finding a beautiful, brand-new gown inside. The inside of the house was completely different from the outside. The walls were covered in light apricot-coloured wallpaper. To the right was a dining room, with a blue-and-white gingham cotton tablecloth spread out, the tabletop spotless. At its centre stood a white porcelain vase holding a bouquet of daisies, the petals covered in dew. To the left was a small sitting room, tightly packed with a greyish-green striped sofa. The sofa faced a brick fireplace, on the mantel of which lay two or three books and a small silver statue of Flora, the goddess of flowers—I had seen it in a magazine once, with a butterfly alighting on her hair. 那是一種奇異的感覺，就像是無意間打開床底落灰的廢舊紙箱，卻發現裡面擺著一件漂亮又嶄新的禮服。房子裡頭跟外面迥然不同。牆面上貼著淺杏色牆紙。右側是一個餐廳，鋪著細碎的藍白格棉桌布，桌面一塵不染，正中心放著一個白瓷瓶，一束雛菊插在裡面，花瓣上沾滿露水。左側是一個小型會客室，被一架灰綠條紋沙發塞得滿滿當當。沙發正對著磚石砌成的壁爐，壁爐上橫放著兩三本書和一個銀色小雕像，是花神芙蘿拉，我在某一本雜誌上看到過，蝴蝶停在她的頭髮上。
"Hello?" This did not look like the home of a leper. 「您好？」這看起來並不像是一個痲瘋病人所住的屋子。
There was still no response. I quietly took one of the daisies, murmured an apology, and turned to leave for home. Just at that moment, I heard a voice from upstairs: "Please come up." It was a beautiful voice—low and powerful, like the sound of a stream striking a rock. It had a magical quality, a mystery that drew one to trace it upstream. It reached out a pair of hands, pulled me back from the door, and pushed me up the stairs. 依舊沒有回應。我悄悄地拿走了一枝雛菊，一邊說著抱歉，一邊轉身，打算出門回家去。就在那一瞬間，我聽到樓上一個聲音說：「請上來吧。」那是個十分好聽的聲音，低沉而有力量，像是溪水撞上礁石的聲音。它有著一股魔力，誘導著他人溯源而上的神秘感。它伸出一雙手，把我從門邊拉了回來，並推著我走上了樓。
"Sorry to disturb you," I said, trying to make my footsteps lighter. 「打擾了。」我努力讓自己腳步變輕。
"Second door on the left." The door was ajar, and warm orange light seeped through the crack. I knocked and entered. Inside, by the desk, sat a man who was smiling at me. 「左手邊第二個房間。」門虛掩著，從門縫裡透出暖橘色的燈光。我敲了敲門，走進去，屋內書桌邊上坐著一個人，正對著我微笑。
I may have been stunned for five seconds, ten seconds, or longer. It was only when I heard him tap his fingernails on the desk that I came back to my senses. Before me sat a gentleman around thirty years old, his legs under the table, his upper body twisted toward me. He had rare blue-violet eyes, like two panes of stained glass in a cathedral window. When moonlight fell from outside, they emitted a quiet, cool radiance. His profile was like an exquisitely crafted sculpture, his golden hair falling to his neck. Seeing that I did not react, he furrowed his brow, tilted his head, and gave me an appraising look. Several strands of golden hair fell lazily around his eyes with the tilt of his head. He rested his elbows on the table, propped his chin on his long fingers, and his rose-coloured lips curved into a smile at just the right angle. 我可能發愣了五秒，十秒，或是更久。在聽見他用指甲輕敲桌面時才回過神來。我面前坐著一位三十歲左右的先生，他的腿放在桌子底下，上半身則扭過來對著我。他有一雙罕見的藍紫色眼睛，像教堂彩窗上鑲著的兩塊玻璃。當月光從外面投射進來時，便發出寧靜又清冷的輝光。他的輪廓像一尊精妙絕倫的雕塑，金髮留到了脖子周圍。見我沒有反應，他皺起眉頭，歪著頭打量了我一下，幾綹金髮隨著他歪頭的角度懶散地垂在他眼睛周圍。他正把胳膊肘支在桌上，用修長的手指托著下巴，玫瑰色的嘴唇隨著笑靨而揚起一個恰到好處的弧度。
"There are no other chairs, sorry," he said with a heavy sigh, pointing to a wooden box by the door. "You can sit there." I thanked him, and he handed me a woollen cushion to put under me. I looked around the room. It was not large, but because there was very little furniture, it did not feel cramped. One wall was covered in butterfly specimens, densely packed together, giving off a dazzling brilliance. Countless bright colours were squeezed and twisted together, flowing down from the specimen wall and spilling into my eyes. I felt that this was a cruel art—the art of capturing and freezing the radiance of life at its final moment. 「沒有其他椅子了，抱歉。」他重重地嘆了口氣，指了指門邊的一口木箱，說，「你可以坐在那裡。」我道了聲謝，他便遞給我一個羊毛軟墊讓我墊在屁股底下。我環顧起這個房間來，它並不能說是寬敞，但因為家具極少，也不顯得擁擠。一面牆上掛滿了蝴蝶標本，它們密密麻麻地排在一起，發出眩目的光彩。無數亮色不斷擠榨，絞緊，從標本牆上流淌下來，落入我的眼睛裡。我感受到這是一門殘忍的藝術，把生命之光匯聚定格在最後一刻的藝術。
"About half an hour before you came in, there was a chubby little boy who wanted to put a bucket of water over my front door." He raised his hand above his head and stretched. "I was making coffee in the kitchen... It's not easy for me to get down the stairs, you know. He ruined my good mood, so I played ghost and scared him away." He winked at me. There was a tear mole under his left eye, which jumped playfully with the movement of his skin. "So, what are you here for?" he asked. 「你進來前大約半個小時，有個小胖子想在我的大門上方放一桶水。」他把手舉過頭頂，伸了個懶腰，「我在廚房泡咖啡……我下樓一趟可不容易，他破壞了我的好心情，所以我扮鬼把他嚇跑了。」他對我輕輕眨了下眼，他的左眼下方有一顆淚痣，隨著皮膚的動作而俏皮地一跳，「所以，你又是來幹什麼的？」他問我。
"That boy asked me to come in... and asked me to bring something back to prove I'd been inside." My voice gradually lost its anchor, growing softer and softer. I hid the daisy in my trouser pocket. 「就是那個孩子讓我進來的……還讓…我帶一樣隨便什麼東西給他，證明我進來過。」我的聲音逐漸失去了著力點，變得越來越小，把雛菊藏在褲子口袋裡。
"Then give him this." He picked up a fine gold chain from the desk and handed it to me. At one end was fastened a translucent, blue-green piece of enamel. "No one else here has one of these." He waved his hand, signalling me to come closer. 「那你就把這個給他吧。」他從桌上拿起一根細細的金鏈條遞給我，一端拴著一片藍綠色透光琺瑯，「這裡沒有別人家會有這個。」他揮揮手，示意我走過去。
I had to admit, I felt ill at ease in his presence. As I approached him, I could smell a scent of butterfly bush about him. I walked to his side, and his eyes looked at me. 我不得不承認，我在他面前感到侷促。當我接近他可以聞到他身上發出一股醉魚草的氣味。我走到他身旁，他的眼睛看著我。

His fingers closed, the tips lightly touching the palm of my hand. He guided my fingers, aimed at the centre of the butterfly, and pressed the pin in with perfect precision. 「看好了。」我能感覺到弗朗西斯湊近了一些，因為他如絲帶般的頭髮正在我後頸上掃掠，彷彿輕而易舉地把我的熱量都集中在那一片皮膚上。他的手指合攏，指尖輕觸著我的掌面。他引導著我的手指，瞄準了那隻蝴蝶的中心，精準無比地把針插了下去。
"Do you think they feel pain?" I knew it was a foolish question. 「你認為它們會痛嗎？」我知道我提了個蠢問題。
Francis had already begun manipulating the butterfly's wings with his tweezers. He stopped what he was doing, tilted his head, and tapped his cheek with the tweezers. After a moment, seeming to have arrived at an answer, he gazed intently into my eyes. 弗朗西斯已經開始在拿鑷子擺弄起那隻蝴蝶的翅膀，他停下了手中的動作，偏著頭，用鑷子輕敲著臉頰。過了一會兒，他似乎有了答案，便重重地凝望著我的眼睛。
"I don't think so," he answered softly. "Rather than rotting in the soil, letting fungi and disgusting lichens crawl over their bodies, I think they would prefer to be remembered like this. I give every butterfly a name. When I mount them, I add their own name below the scientific name... When they are sent into various people's hands, no one may care, but I will remember them. I have a good memory—I can even remember the meanings of their names..." His face flushed slightly. He placed his right hand over his chest. "The other day, I found this butterfly under an orchid. There was an ant crawling on its wing... Soon it would have been disassembled into several small pieces and carried off into the dark underground." 「我認為不會。」他輕柔地回答道，「比起在泥土裡腐爛，讓真菌和噁心的地衣爬滿它們的身體，它們應該會更喜歡這樣被人記住。我會給每個隻蝴蝶都起名字，當我把它們做成標本後會在學名下綴上它們自己的名字……當它們被送到各種人手裡時可能卻沒有人會在意，但是我會記住它們，我的記性很好，我甚至能記住它們名字的含義…」他的臉色微微發紅，右手放在胸口上，「前兩天我在一株蘭花下發現了這隻蝴蝶，當時它的翅膀上爬了一隻螞蟻…過不了多久它就會被拆分成數個小塊運往黑暗的地下。」
"What do you think, Artie?" His eyes suddenly drew close to mine, our foreheads nearly touching. His gaze blazed with intense heat. "How rich and vibrant must a person's life be, to face without flinching the illness and suffering that time brings?" As usual, he did not expect an answer. He bent his head again and began manipulating the butterfly he had rescued from the ant's jaws, murmuring to himself, "I plan to name it Aurora, because its wings remind me of the northern lights." 「你覺得，亞蒂。」他的眼睛突然貼近了我的眼睛，我們的額頭幾乎擦在了一起，他的眼中迸射出熾熱的光，「一個人的生命要多豐富有生機，才能坦然奔赴時光翩躚所帶來的病痛與折磨？」和往常一樣，他並不期待一個回答，他又開始低頭擺弄起那隻被他從螞蟻嘴裡拯救出來的鳳蝶，並自言自語道，「我打算給它取名叫 Aurora，因為它的翅膀讓我聯想到極光。」
"Is there one named Arthur?" I asked him. 「有沒有叫做亞瑟的？」我問他。
He looked at me and said, with unusual firmness, "No. But perhaps for now." That evening, I received a call from my mother. She told me she had found a good job in London and had signed a six-month contract. 他看著我，異常堅決地說，「沒有，但也許是暫時的。」晚上，我接到了母親的電話，她表示她在倫敦謀得了一份好差事，已經簽了半年的合同。
◇
I gained more time alone with Francis, yet those hours were all too brief—'time flits by,' as he had said. I spent less than a year with him, yet it felt as though I had poured all the fullest, most vivid emotions of my life into that tall, dilapidated tower in a small town in Arrochar. Spring lingered for a moment on the petals of a flower, then was carried away by passing insects. Summer arrived quickly. My life broke free of its frame along with the fickle rains. I felt that the only joy in my day was seeing Francis. 我獲得了更多與弗朗西斯獨處的時間，但這些時間卻也太短暫，如他所說的，「時光翩躚」。我與他相伴不過一年不到，卻彷彿把我人生中最飽滿，最鮮活的情感，都傾注在了阿羅赫爾某個小鎮中那座高大破敗的塔上。春天短促地在某一株花的花瓣上停留了一下，接著就被過往的昆蟲馱走。夏天很快就來了，我的生活隨著陰晴不定的雨一同脫離框架，我覺得我一天之內唯一的樂事就是見到弗朗西斯。
"Why do you never go out?" At that moment, he was sitting on the bed, and I was lying on his lap. We were looking through his photo album. He had travelled to many places, including some whose names I had never even heard. 「你為什麼永遠不出門？」那時他坐在床上，我躺在他的大腿上，我們正翻看著他的相冊，他曾經去過很多地方，包括一些我連名字都沒有聽過的地方。
The hand turning the pages stopped in mid-air. His thin eyebrows furrowed slightly. After a long moment, he answered, "I don't think the people in this town would want to see me." 他翻頁的手頓在了空中，兩道細眉輕輕擰在一起。過了半晌，他回答我說：「我覺得這小鎮裡的人可能不太希望見到我。」
"Same for me," I said, rubbing my face with my hands. "I was born in a so-called big city, but our family lived under a bridge arch, surviving on the rotten vegetables my father could grab with his meagre wages. That was my life. In that city, other children would call me a little beggar without a second thought... But after moving here, I became a pampered young gentleman in everyone else's eyes. Prejudice about one's origins always limits the distance between people, even when the stereotype doesn't fit the person... Maybe that's why I can't get along well with others." He said nothing, only reached out his hand, gently brushed the edge of my ear, and tucked the stray hairs at my temple behind it. 「對我也一樣。」我把雙手放在臉上蹭了蹭，「我出生在一個所謂的大城市，但我們一家只住在橋洞底下，靠著我父親微薄的工資搶購剩下的那點爛蔬菜。這就是我的生活，在原先那座城市，其他孩子會毫不在乎地叫我小乞丐…可到了這兒之後，我就在他人眼中變成了一位養尊處優的少爺。對出身的成見總會限制人與人之間的距離，即使那個刻板印象與本人並不相符…可能這就是我無法與其他人很好相處的原因。」他沒有說話，只是伸出手，輕輕擦過我的耳廓，把我鬢角的碎髮攏到耳後去了。
"So I prefer to lie on the earthen hill behind the town and daydream." I stretched my arms upward, looking at my fingertips. "This time of year, the bellflowers should be blooming. I plan to go have a look tonight." I secretly watched his expression, and asked tentatively, "Will you come, Francis?" The expression on his face suddenly froze. His eyes, which on ordinary days barely ever stirred with emotion, were seized by a childlike joy. I saw his lips tremble; his soul seemed about to break free of his body and dance right there. But he said nothing, only nodded quietly. 「所以，我更喜歡躺在鎮子後面那個土包子上發呆。」我向上伸了個懶腰，看著自己的指尖，「這個季節，桔梗應該開花了，我打算晚上去看看。」我悄悄觀察著他臉上的表情，試探地問了一句，「你來嗎？弗朗西斯。」他臉上的表情突然凝滯，他那平日裡幾乎從來沒有因為任何事情而掀起波瀾的眼睛被一種天真的喜悅所佔領，我看見他的嘴唇顫抖不已，他的靈魂彷彿要脫出軀殼在此地起舞。但他一句話也沒有說，只是靜靜地點了點頭。
"Then I'll wait for you," I said, squinting at him with a smile. 「那我等你。」我瞇起眼睛對他笑了笑。
◇
Never had I longed so much for nightfall. I began waiting from the afternoon onward. I sat at my window, watching the afterglow dart through the gaps in the clouds, reflecting an oil-painting orange-pink, until a giant palette knife scraped across it, leaving only a deep black-blue on the canvas. I put on my coat and left the house, walking along the gravel path to the back of the hill. Fireflies paused to rest along the lines of grass by the roadside. Some rose in pairs, like two planets revolving around each other. I thought I had arrived early enough, but Francis was already there waiting. He stood with his back against a tree, his golden hair loosely tied at the right side of his neck. His coat hung down to his knees, and in his left hand he held a wooden cane. 我從沒有一天那麼期盼黑夜來臨。我從午後就開始等，我坐在窗前，看著餘暉在雲層的縫隙間穿梭，折射出油畫質地的橘粉色，接著被一把巨大的調色刀刮過，只留下畫布上一片深邃的黑藍。我穿好外套離開家門，走在通往後山的碎石子路上，螢火蟲順著路邊青草的紋路停駐小憩，有的成雙飛起來，像兩顆相互繞轉的行星。我自以為自己來得夠早，可弗朗西斯已經等在了那裡，他背靠著一棵樹，金髮在右耳側鬆散地紮起來。他的風衣垂到膝蓋附近，左手拿著一根木質拐杖。
"You came early," I said, sitting down at the base of the tree and looking up at him. 「你來早了。」我走過去，在樹底坐下，仰起頭對他說。
"Mm," he replied, holding the tree trunk and silently sitting down beside me. 「嗯。」他回答道，扶著樹幹，默默在我身邊坐下。
My ears were filled with the sound of cicadas, which did not seem harsh at that moment. We gazed at the soft clouds in the sky, watching the evening wind curl them into strange shapes. The stars shone like diamonds. The night sky felt very close, as if we could reach out and touch it. The unremarkable weeds glistened with a bright light, stretching into the distance. 我的耳邊充斥著蟬鳴，它們此刻並不顯得聒噪。我們望著天邊的軟雲，看晚風把它們捲起，形成各種奇異的形狀。繁星像鑽石一樣閃耀，夜空離我們很近，彷彿伸伸手就能觸碰到。不起眼的雜草閃著晶亮的光，一直延伸到遠處。
"It seems the bellflowers haven't bloomed yet," I said, leaning back against the tree trunk. The woody fragrance seized my senses in an instant. 「桔梗好像並沒有開花。」我說，向後靠在了樹幹上，木質的清香在一瞬間攫取了我的感官。
"No," he said, raising his hand and naturally draping it over my shoulder. "Perhaps they will take a few more weeks." 「是啊。」他抬起手，自然地攬過我的肩膀，「可能還要過幾個星期。」
"I was too early," I said, lowering my head and playing with the tips of the grass. Through the dark sky, I seemed to see time gathering beyond the night into a thin thread, stretching toward the eternal end of the universe. I pressed my cheek against Francis's shoulder. The sound of his breathing rose and fell. 「我預判早了。」我低下頭，玩弄著草尖。透過深色的天空，我彷彿看見時間在夜空以外匯聚成一道細線，向永恆的宇宙盡頭延伸。我把臉貼在弗朗西斯的肩膀上，他呼吸的聲音升起又落下。
"Arthur," he suddenly turned his head, and his lips inadvertently brushed quickly against my forehead. 「亞瑟。」他突然轉過頭，嘴唇不經意間快速擦過了我的額頭。
"What?" I sat up, and the palms of my hands began to sweat. 「什麼？」我坐起來，掌心開始冒汗。
Again he gazed into my eyes for a long time, his lips pressed tightly together, his blue-violet pupils glowing with an unusually dazzling light in the thick night. On his face I saw a deep pain, which had formed a pale mask tightly stuck to his features. He took my hand, then let it go, then took it again, tightly. 他又久久凝望著我的眼睛，雙唇緊抿，藍紫瞳孔在濃重的夜色中發出異常奪目的光。我在他臉上看到了深刻的痛苦，它們化成一張蒼白的面具，緊緊地黏在他的臉上。他拉住我的手，又放開，又再次緊緊拉住。
"There will be no moon tonight," he said after a long silence, suddenly smiling at me. 「今晚註定沒有月亮。」他沉默了許久，突然對我微笑道。
We did not speak for the next few hours. He indicated that he wanted to sit a while longer, so I rose and bade him goodnight. As I made my way down the hill, I turned my head to look at him. He stood leaning askew against the tree, his silhouette outlined by the night, solemn and still. He lifted his head, gazing off into some unknown distance. 後來幾個小時我們都不再說話，他表示還想再坐一會，於是我起身向他道晚安。我在下山路上扭頭看他，他斜倚著樹站著，夜色勾勒出他肅穆的剪影。他昂著頭，不知望向何方。
◇
Near the end of summer, my mother returned from London. She wore powder, a cinched-waist dress, and had cut her hair short. She looked as young as the wedding photograph I remembered from my early childhood—even younger than that. She brought with her a well-dressed man, my stepfather. As soon as she entered, she urged me to pack my things. We would leave this town, leave our poor and destitute life, and embrace a brand new, golden world. My stepfather drove a car, which he parked outside our shabby little house. He did not mind that my mother was a widow, nor did he mind my existence. They lived quite happily together for the next several decades. My mother lived in her favourite fairy tale, and I was sincerely happy for her. 夏季快結束的時候，我母親從倫敦回來了，她抹著脂粉，穿著束腰洋裝，還剪了短髮，她又變得像那張我很小時見到的結婚照一樣年輕，甚至比那時更加年輕。她帶來一位衣著光鮮的男人，那是我的繼父。她一進門就催促著我收拾東西，我們將離開這個小鎮，離開過去窮困潦倒的生活，擁抱一個全新的，金碧輝煌的世界。我的繼父開著一輛轎車，停在我們破敗的小屋門口。他不介意我母親是個寡婦，也不介意我的存在。他們在今後直到的幾十年裡都生活得相當幸福。我母親生活在她最愛的童話裡，我也由衷地為她感到喜悅。
We left one morning. My mother and stepfather were in the room talking with the landlord. I sat in my stepfather's car, fiddling with the enamel fragment on my neck—perhaps the only thing I could take with me from that dream. My gaze unconsciously drifted to the house across the way. The door slowly opened. Francis, using his cane for support, staggered out. He placed a box wrapped in parchment paper on the doorstep, gave a deep look in my direction, and offered a fleeting smile. Then he furrowed his brow and stood there for a long time. Finally, he closed the door and went back inside. I sneaked out of the car, picked up the package he had left at the door, and opened it back in the car. Inside was a pinewood picture frame. Firmly fixed in the centre was a false butterfly, its dark green wings letting a faint green shadow fall onto the white background. The edges of the wings were inlaid with fine gold wire. Below it was pinned a small card, the handwriting as light as a butterfly. 我們在一個清晨離開，我母親與繼父在房間裡與房東交流，我坐在我繼父的車裡，撥弄著脖子上的琺瑯碎片，可能那是我能從夢中帶走的唯一東西了。我的目光還是下意識地看向對面，那扇門卻漸漸地打開了，弗朗西斯以拐杖作為支撐點，踉踉蹌蹌地走了出來，把一個羊皮紙包著的盒子放在門口，深深地朝我這邊看了一眼，他揚起一個轉瞬即逝的微笑，又皺緊了眉頭，他站了好一會兒，最後關門回去。我偷偷跑下車，拿走了他門口的那個紙包，回到車裡打開，裡面放著一個松木畫框，中央牢牢地釘著一隻假蝴蝶，微光透過它深綠色的翅膀，在白布底上印下一個淡淡的綠影。翅膀邊緣鑲著一圈細金絲。它的下方釘著一枚小小的名片，字跡輕盈得就像一隻蝴蝶。
"Arthur," he had written. 「Arthur.」他寫道。
My mother returned soon after. I tucked the package into my suitcase. My stepfather got into the car and began telling me about our travel plans. He intended to drive all the way back to London, showing us the scenery along the way. The following year, he would send me to school. 我的母親很快就回來了，我把紙包收進行李箱裡。我的繼父也上了車，向我敘述著接下來的旅行計劃，他打算一路開回倫敦去，帶我們看看沿途的風景。等到下一年再送我去學校。
I still held the enamel necklace. His voice gradually faded into a blur in my ears. The farmhouses on either side flew past in reverse. I thought of whose milk bottle had not been collected, whose eggs had not yet been delivered—but those regrets, too, like the scenery on both sides, travelled away from me in a hazy greyish-yellow. 我仍舊握著那枚琺瑯項鍊，他的聲音逐漸在我耳中模糊了。兩旁的農舍景象飛速向後退去，我想起了誰家的牛奶瓶沒收，誰家的雞蛋還沒來得及送，但這些遺憾也逐漸像兩旁的景物一樣，在一片朦朧的灰黃中與我背道而行。
I tried to write to Francis. Even now, I can still recite his address from memory. But he never replied. 我試著給弗朗西斯寫信，我到現在依舊能背熟他家的地址，但他從未回信過。
My stepfather gave me the finest education. After graduating from university, I became an entomologist, as I had wished. The chain of the necklace gradually aged and finally broke during a bath. I picked it up and stored it with the specimen case under the bed. Only when I retired did I hang the specimen case at my bedside. The necklace itself, however, was discovered by my young adopted daughter and lost forever. 我的繼父讓我接受了最好的教育，我在大學畢業後如願成為了一名昆蟲學家。那根項鍊的鏈條逐漸老化，終於在某一次洗澡時斷裂。我把它撿起來，與標本匣一起，收在床底。直到我退休時才把標本掛在床頭，而那枚項鍊被我年幼的養女無意間發現，便永遠地失去了。
When I was twenty-five, I returned to Arrochar, only to be told that the house had changed owners and the original occupant had disappeared. I increasingly questioned the reality of that year. Had he been merely a ghost from another world in my dreams? I did not even know his surname—until a year later, when I received a letter. 在我二十五歲時曾回阿羅赫爾過一次，卻被告知那座房子已經易了主，原屋主不知去向。我越來越懷疑那一年的真實性，他是否只是我夢中另一個世界的幽靈？我甚至不知道他的姓氏，直到我一年後收到一封信。
It was morning again. The postman knocked on my door and handed me a thin envelope. The moment my fingers touched that letter, my heart suddenly began to race, and an indescribable itch rose beneath my skin. On that cold morning, I stood at my doorstep in my pyjamas and opened the letter I would remember forever: 那也是個清晨，郵差先生敲響了我家的門，把一個薄薄的信封遞給我。當我的手指觸到那封信的那一刻我的心突然跳得很快，皮膚之下升起一股難以言喻的瘙癢。在那個寒冷的清晨，我站在門口，穿著睡衣，拆開了這封讓我永遠銘記的信：
Dear Artie: 親愛的亞蒂：
It's me. I think it's time I formally introduced myself. My name is Francis Bonnefoy, of the Bonnefoy family—a distinguished shipbuilding dynasty in France. My father holds the majority of shares, and as the eldest son, great expectations were placed upon me from birth. But I never seemed to belong to them. I would paint on the monotonous grey hulls of ships, skip class to see the scenery, once fell into a sewer trying to pick a wildflower. They seemed to notice this too. My father gave up on me early on and focused his attention on raising my younger brother. He and I were never close. I was treated as dispensable. Before important social occasions, as the time to leave drew near, they would ask me, 'Francis, why don't you not come this time?' At twenty, I entered university to study art. I was once a member of the football team. One day during practice, I suddenly collapsed because my legs had gone as weak as cotton. Later, I was diagnosed with Charcot disease. My muscles are wasting away faster than I could have imagined. I don't know how much longer I have to live. 是我，我想是時候向你正式地介紹一下我自己了，我叫弗朗西斯·波諾弗瓦，隸屬於那個法國嚴謹的船塢世家，波諾弗瓦家族。我的父親持有最多的股份，而身為長子的我一出生就被寄予厚望。但我似乎不屬於他們，我會在單調的灰色船體上畫畫，我會逃課出去看風景，我曾經為了摘一朵野花而掉進下水道裡去。他們似乎也發現了這一點，我的父親盡早放棄了我，轉而培養我的弟弟。他與我的感情並不深厚。我被視為可有可無的存在，在一些重要的社交場合，臨行前他們總會問我：「弗朗西斯，你別來了吧？」我二十歲時進入大學學習藝術，我曾是足球隊的一員。一次，我在練習時突然摔倒，因為我的雙腿像棉花一樣軟弱無力。後來，我被診斷為夏科病，我的肌肉正以意想不到的速度萎縮，我不知道還能活多久。
After falling ill, the world suddenly grew much larger. I took the money my family gave me for treatment and travelled around the world. Each time I arrived somewhere new, the endless emptiness inside me diminished a little. I thought that rather than living like a year on a sickbed, it would be better to die quietly in beautiful places. At twenty-three, I arrived in Arrochar. My body was growing weaker, so I settled there. The town was indeed picturesque. There were many wildflowers in my yard, and in summer they would attract countless butterflies to dance around. I thought they were very much like me. In their brief, dazzling lives, they are accompanied only by beauty. They are romantic insects. I suppose that was when I grew to like them. 在我得病後世界突然變得大了起來，我拿著家人給我的治療費用去環遊了世界。每到一個地方，我內心無盡的空虛就消弭了幾許。我覺得與其在病床上度日如年，不如在美麗的地方悄無聲息地死去。二十三歲時我到了阿羅赫爾，我的身體也越來越沒有力氣，於是我安頓了下來。這座小鎮確實風光旖旎，我的院子裡有很多雜花，一到夏天就會吸引無數隻蝴蝶前來起舞。我覺得它們跟我真像，在她們短暫而絢爛的一生中只有繁華相伴，這是一種浪漫的昆蟲。我猜我就是那時喜歡上它們的。
Then seven years passed. I still knew very little about the villagers. Any child who tried to play tricks would be scared away by me. I had heard the interesting rumours about myself, but I did not care much about them. 後來過了七年，我依舊對村莊裡的人知之甚少。惡作劇的孩子都會被我嚇跑，我也知道關於我的有趣流言，但我並不怎麼在意。
That night, when you entered my house, you opened a door to the outside world for me. I do not know why I let you in. Perhaps it can be attributed to fate. I was extraordinarily happy each time you visited. I felt once again the satisfaction of being needed. But I was also afraid to show my feelings, for after all, I possessed only a broken body and a lonely, eccentric soul. 那天晚上你進了我的房子，就為我打開了一扇通往外界的門。我不知道我為什麼會讓你進來，也許可以把它歸結為命運。你每次來訪我都異常開心，我又有了一種被人需要的滿足感。但我也怯於表露自己的情感，畢竟我只擁有一副不完整的身體，和一個孤獨怪異的靈魂。
That summer night, you said I had arrived early. In truth, I had spent the entire afternoon moving myself from the house to that hillside. My limbs no longer seemed to belong to me. Time digs a piece out of me every day, and I must bow my head to it. I still remember the incongruous smell of hair gel when you leaned your head on my shoulder. I deliberately brushed my lips against your forehead when I turned my head. I hoped you would notice. That was my farewell. 那個夏夜你說我到得很早，實際上我花了一下午把自己從家裡挪到那個山坡上，我的四肢彷彿不再屬於我自己了。時光每日都從我身上挖去一塊，我不得不向它低首。我仍然記得你靠在我肩膀上時那股格格不入的髮膠味道，我在轉頭時故意擦過了你的額頭。我希望你能注意到，那是我的告別。
It took me half the night to get home again. I twisted gold thread and enamel into a butterfly. No butterfly can compare to your uniqueness. I hope you have received it. I know you saw me that morning. Forgive me for not speaking to you. The purpose of this specimen is no longer to be remembered, Artie—but to make you remember me. 我又花了半個深夜回家，用金絲和琺瑯擰成一隻蝴蝶，沒有一種蝴蝶能比得上你的獨特。我希望你已經收到了，我知道你在那個清晨看見我了，原諒我沒有與你說話。這個標本的作用已經不再是讓它被記住，亞蒂，而是讓你記住我。
My condition is deteriorating rapidly. I have difficulty breathing from time to time now. I imagine it will not be long before I end my brief life. But I have lived this life fully, Artie—especially since I met you. 我的病情急速惡化，現在時不時呼吸困難，想必過不了多久我就得結束我短暫的人生了。不過這一世我過得很豐富，亞蒂，尤其是當我遇見了你。
How I long to say I love you, Arthur. I want to look into your dark green eyes and say, I love you. 多想說我愛你，亞瑟，我想當面望著你深綠色的眼睛說，我愛你。
To this day, I still dare not recall him properly. But since I am soon to depart this life, it will not hurt to think of him. Francis—he seemed the kind of person who, once he thought of something, would carry it out without fail. Even a momentary spark of passion was precious to him. He never regretted his decisions, because he believed that life always holds its sighs. Like a fool, on some winter or spring morning, he would pack his bags and set out on another open road, walking freely among thorns and flowers. Every path was worth exploring to him, because every path seemed to have no end. 直到今天，我仍舊不敢好好地回憶他，不過既然我即將長逝，想想他便已無妨。弗朗西斯，他似乎屬於那種想到了什麼就一定會去落實到底的行動家。哪怕一剎那燃起的激情對他來說都珍貴異常。他從不為自己的決定後悔，因為他相信人生總有唏噓。他像愚者一般，總在某個冬日或春日的早晨背上行囊踏上另一條坦途，自由地行走於荊棘與花叢之間。每一條路對他來說都值得探索，因為每一條路對他來說都看不見盡頭。
Francis. He made every spring of mine smell of fried eggs and burnt toast. My nose remembers the scent of butterfly bush. I habitually dilute my hair gel with water. I pause for a long time before flower beds, just to see clearly whether the butterfly with blue-glowing wings is a glasswing. 弗朗西斯，他使我的每個春天都散發著煎雞蛋和焦吐司的味道，我的鼻腔記住了醉魚草的香味，我習慣性往髮膠裡面灌水，我在花叢前久久駐足，只為看清那隻翅膀閃著藍色螢光的蝴蝶到底是不是琉璃鳳蝶。
Francis. When I was young, I often discussed with myself the nature of my relationship with him. He was a presence that fit just right—always hovering at the threshold of every kind of relationship, rarely crossing over. Teacher, friend, lover. Now, as I age with time, I have come to understand many things. Francis, Francis. From the moment I met him, that name ceased to be just a few letters that anyone could possess and became the most beautiful string of notes. It became concrete. One moment it was still passing between my lips and tongue, and the next, its owner seemed to stride across the night and rush toward me like a gale. When I lie in bed and speak his name, I can feel his golden hair falling on the back of my neck, his hand gently holding mine, guiding me as I drive a brass pin into the back of a glasswing butterfly. 弗朗西斯，我年輕時曾多次與我自己探討我與他的關係，他是一個恰到好處的存在，他始終漂浮在每一層關係的臨界點上，很少越過去，亦師亦友亦情人。現在當我隨著時光老去，我倒是想通了很多事。弗朗西斯，弗朗西斯。從認識他的那一刻起，這個名字就從任何人皆可擁有的幾個字母變成了一串最美妙的音符。它變得具象化，上一秒它還在我的唇舌間流轉，下一秒它的主人就彷彿踏過夜色，狂風一般朝我襲來。躺在床上念這個名字時，我能感覺到他的金髮垂落在我的脖頸後側，他的手輕輕地握住我的手，引導我把銅釘釘在一隻琉璃燕蝶的背上。
I have come to understand, perhaps, what Francis means to me. He represents the aurora at the equator, the larch at the South Pole, snow in summer. He is an illogical dream, a beautiful fallacy. 我大約明白了弗朗西斯對我而言代表了什麼，它代表著赤道的極光，南極點的落葉松，夏季的雪。他就是一個不合常理的幻夢，一個美麗的謬論。
Francis, who gave you permission to leave me without warning, to deceive me, so that I have had to spend my entire life trying to catch up with your aging steps? 弗朗西斯，是誰准許你擅自離開我，矇騙我，使我要花費一生的時間追上你老去的步伐？
