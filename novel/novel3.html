A letter started all at once 一封倏然起筆的信

Once, while working as a stewardess, I encountered an elderly gentleman. He boarded at London, around five in the morning. He was lean, with a ramrod-straight back, dressed in a long camel-coloured trench coat and square-toed leather shoes, topped with a flat cap—an outdated ensemble that somehow looked dashing on him. His hair was mostly white, but his eyes were a striking green—though they seemed always to gaze through a veil of mist, making his look perpetually sorrowful and blurred. The corners of his mouth habitually turned downward, his upper lip pressing against the lower in a stubborn, almost contrary manner. As soon as he boarded, he switched on a dim lamp—so faint it seemed about to expire—and by that flickering, dying light, he began to write. He wrote for a long time. As naturally as ice melting in warmth, his brows slowly relaxed, his eyes pouring forth an infinite, tender radiance, even curling into a smile. Yet his ending was barren and desolate, like a west wind sweeping across the grey moors of Scotland. Finally, he crumpled the fine sheets of paper and tossed them into the waste bin at his feet. For the rest of the journey, he stared out the window. When he disembarked, he leaned on a cane—his left leg seemed slightly lame. I quietly rescued the crumpled paper from the pile of fruit peels, cigarette butts, and chewing gum. He had written a long letter. The salutation was written and crossed out, crossed out and rewritten, but the words "Francis" were still legible—in elegant cursive. The handwriting that followed was as precise and neat as his attire: 我當乘務員時遇到過一位老人。他從倫敦上車，那時是凌晨五點左右。他身材很瘦，脊背挺直，穿咖色長風衣和方頭皮鞋，戴平頂帽，早已過時了的打扮在他身上顯得風度翩翩；他的頭髮已經白了大半，卻有一雙漂亮的綠眼睛；但彷彿一直隱在霧氣後面看人，因而眼神總是悲傷又朦朧；他的嘴角習慣於向下撇著，上嘴唇跟下嘴唇較勁，於是顯得有些固執而彆扭。他一上車就開了一盞微弱得像是快嚥氣的燈，藉著苟延殘喘的燈光寫什麼東西，他寫了很久。就好像冰塊遇暖消融那麼自然，剛開始寫時，他的眉頭緩慢舒展開，眼中傾注出無限溫柔的光輝，甚至勾起一個微笑。然而他的結尾卻荒蕪又蒼涼，像西風掃過蘇格蘭灰色的原野。最後他把那幾張質量極好的紙揉皺了，扔在腳邊的垃圾桶裡。餘下的時間他望向車窗外頭，他下車的時候拄著一根拐杖，左腿似乎有些跛。我悄悄從一堆果皮、菸頭、口香糖中搶救出那團紙。他寫了一封長信，稱謂寫了又劃，劃了又寫，但可以辨認出「弗朗西斯」四個字，漂亮的花體字，接下來的筆跡像他的裝束一樣，嚴謹而工整：
You must have realised it has been a long time since I last came to see you. 你應該已經意識到我很久沒來看你了。
Last time I came, summer had only just begun to stir. I took the train—4:32 in the morning—from London to Plymouth. The carriage was nearly empty. The train crawled along; I could hear small stones tumbling beneath the tracks, shattering like porcelain shards. How long did it last? Minutes? Hours? An invisible current seemed to wash over the sky, light seeping little by little into the pitch-black canopy. A few miles away, grey-green mountains loomed, crowded together like caviar. Closer to hand, listless weeds were scattered with small, pale blue flowers—forget-me-nots, perhaps, the kind that delight in their own company. Occasionally I would see one, or several, neat vegetable plots, growing carrots or celery, which from a distance looked like oily, gleaming olivines—flourishing as if no one had ever tended them. 上次我來時夏季剛剛伸出觸角。我坐車，凌晨四點三十二分，從倫敦到普利茅斯。車廂裡沒有什麼人，車開得很慢，我聽見小石子在鐵軌下翻滾，發出瓷片碎掉的聲音。過了多久？幾分鐘？幾小時？似乎有一條無形的細流沖洗著天空，光亮一點點滲進濃黑色的天幕，幾英里的遠處聳立著灰綠色的群山，像魚子醬一樣密密麻麻地擠在一起；近處是沒什麼精神的雜草，中間撒著一些淺藍色的花，大約是勿忘我一類愛自得其樂的花朵；偶爾會看到一塊，或者幾塊整整齊齊的菜畦，種著胡蘿蔔或西芹，遠望去只剩一塊又油又亮的橄欖石，彷彿沒有人照料也長得那麼好。
I was only in my thirties then, though I already felt ancient—old enough to gulp down all the pain in the world with a mouthful of rum. I played sudoku to pass the time and to soothe my frayed nerves. My legs wouldn't stop trembling. The pencil grew damp in my palm, and I wiped it carelessly on my trousers. It was a game I had known since I was eight, yet I couldn't fill in a single number. In the end, I tore the entire newspaper to shreds and threw it in the bin. 我才三十多歲，雖然當時已經覺得自己年紀很大了，大到可以把一切痛苦都就著蘭姆酒一口喝下的時候了。我玩數獨遊戲消磨時間，順便安撫自己繃起來的神經。我的雙腿一直發抖，鉛筆被手心攥得濕漉漉的，我在褲子上胡亂擦了幾下。明明是我從八歲起就熟識的遊戲，我卻一個數字也填不出來，最後我把那整份報紙都連帶著撕碎扔進垃圾桶。
London is now in the depths of winter; the sun rarely shows its face. One grey day follows another, the rain as sharp as knives. On every overcast day, my left knee aches and throbs—as if a little man were hammering at my kneecap. I remember being thirteen, the two of us stark naked, leaping from a high bank into a murky pond. My left knee struck a hard stone at the bottom—I suppose that's when the trouble began. My wife and children urged me to move with them to Bolzano, where the sun shines and winters are mild. But I stayed. I told them, 'My sun has already set.' 倫敦現在正值冬季，太陽很少有露臉的機會。陰天一個接著一個，雨水像刀子一樣鋒利。每到陰天我左邊的膝蓋就又酸又疼，好像有小人拿鎚子敲我的膝蓋骨。我記起十三歲跟你光著身子，從幾米高的岸邊跳進一個陰暗的池塘。我的左膝磕在水底的硬卵石上，大約那時候落下了病根。我的孩子和妻子勸我和他們一起搬去博爾扎諾住，說那裡常常艷陽高照，冬季也不冷，但我留了下來，跟他們說「我的太陽已經下山了」。
Yes, I married. I hope that news won't bring you to tears. My wife is a mixed-race woman from Marseille. We met at a blind date, both of us already dulled and numbed by years of solitary labour, by the fog and the seasons fading from saturation, by the bells of Big Ben marking day and night. We sat across from each other like two scarecrows gazing across a cornfield. There was a pungent smell of saffron about her. She made excellent fish soup. Her eyes were a violet blue. We have three children. One of them has eyes clearer than topaz, and golden hair that smells of wheat. I named him Francis. My wife had no objection—just as I never asked why she named our other son Bernard. Between us there exists a certain ineffable, wordless understanding. 沒錯，我結婚了，希望你聽到不會掉眼淚。我的妻子是個生在馬賽的混血，我們相識於一次無關緊要的相親，那時我們都已在一人的操勞中、在迷濛的霧氣和褪去飽和度的四季裡，被大本鐘宣判日夜的鐘聲敲打得遲鈍而麻木。我們坐在彼此對面，像兩個稻草人隔著玉米田遙望。她的身上有一股辛辣的西紅花味道，她很會做魚湯，她有一雙藍得發紫的眼睛。我們一共有三個孩子，其中一個孩子，他的眼眸比托帕石還清澈，金髮散發出麥子的香氣，我給他取名叫弗朗西斯。我的妻子對此毫無異議，就像我從不過問她為什麼要給我們的另一個男孩子取名叫伯納德一樣，我們之間存在著某種詞不達意的默契。
It is winter now. Perhaps that is why the view outside the window is a swiftly moving greyish-yellow, with a white lace border in the middle distance—snow that has fallen on withered grass. Two days ago, a great snow came. When I boarded the train, I saw a few sparrows near the tracks. Their pale yellow beaks were like the tip of a star atop a Christmas tree. A whistle sounded in the distance. One of them fluttered its tiny wings, chirped, turned its head, and flew away. The others followed. On the snow, a line of tiny claw prints remained. I haven't seen such vivid colours in a long time. Perhaps my eyes are failing. In recent years, my eyesight has become wretched—I always feel there are mosquitoes flitting before my eyes. I don't know where the sharp eyesight we had when we shot arrows in the countryside thirty-odd years ago has gone. Even if you stood before me, I would have to lean in to within a few inches to recognise those irises of your laughing eyes. Trains run much faster now than in those days. Everyone can lean comfortably back in their leather seats, gazing through clear, flawless glass at the scenery passing by without hindrance. These years, everything has risen: prices, buildings, prosperity, restlessness, anger... When I walk up Kopsius Lane, I can no longer find the three-penny roses you used to give me. 現在是冬天，不知是不是因為這個原因，窗外是一片高速流動的灰黃色，視野中央是一條白色的花邊，應該是覆蓋在枯草上的積雪。前天剛下過一場大雪，我上車時看到鐵軌附近落了幾隻麻雀，牠們嫩黃色的喙像聖誕樹頂上星星的一角。遠處傳來汽笛聲，其中一隻拍打著細小的翅膀，叫了幾聲，一扭頭就飛走了，其他的也跟著飛去了，雪地上留下一排細小的爪印。我很久沒見過這麼鮮亮的顏色了，可能是我眼睛出了問題，這幾年我的眼睛糟糕透了，總感覺有蚊子在我眼前飛來飛去，不知道三十多年前我們在鄉下射箭時的視力被丟到哪個角落去了。就算你站在我面前，我也得湊近幾十公分，才能認出你那雙鳶尾色的笑眼。現在的列車比那時跑得快上不少，因為人人都已經能夠舒舒服服地靠在敞篷車的皮質座椅上，透過空明潔淨的水晶玻璃，無拘無束地欣賞沿途的風景了。這幾年什麼都在漲：樓層、物價、繁榮、浮躁、憤怒……我走過科普斯山路時再也買不到你以前送給我的那種三便士一枝的玫瑰花了。
The carriage is very empty. A few young people are gathered together playing bridge. A young woman, wrapped in a white headscarf with a dark green border, her shawl trailing long tassels, hums a soft, nameless tune. A child sleeps in her arms. It is the off-season for the seaside; no one but me would be carrying a suitcase from London all the way to Brest at this hour. Brest—is that the name? And between, one must cross a cold channel of sea. Yet it was only a channel that separated us then. I returned to London at twenty. You saw me off at the harbour. As the ship pulled away, I leaned over the railing and waved. The wind was strong. Your hair—those frivolous long locks you insisted on keeping—was blown across your face. You looked ridiculous. 車廂裡很空，有幾個年輕人聚在一堆打橋牌，一位年輕女士裹著白底墨綠邊的頭巾，披肩上墜著長長的流蘇，輕輕哼唱一曲不知名的小調，她的懷裡孩子在熟睡。現在是去海邊的淡季，沒人會像我一樣大清早拎著皮箱從倫敦一路向布雷斯特進軍的。布雷斯特，是這個名字嗎？中間還要跨過一道冰冷的海峽。而那時撕裂我們的也只有一道海峽。我二十歲時回到倫敦，你到港口送我，船開時我趴在欄杆上向你招手，海風很大，你被你執意留著的輕浮長髮糊了一臉，模樣很滑稽。
"I'll come see you, Arthur!" 「我會來看你的，亞瑟！」
The moment you opened your mouth, the wind blew your hair inside. You pushed it aside and kept shouting, until every angle of my vision had closed its doors to you. Only then did your voice fade from my ears. 你剛張嘴頭髮就飛進你的嘴裡，你又用手把它撥開，接著繼續扯著嗓子大喊，直到我的每個視角都對你閉起門扉，你的喊聲才從我耳中散去。
I should have taken a photograph of you looking so foolish. But even if I had, there would be no chance to show it off to you now. We have only one photograph. It lies before me now. My expression is sour, rolling my eyes at the hand you've placed on my shoulder. You are laughing, holding my hand, urging me to take more. 我真該把你那時的醜樣子拍下來，可是就算拍下來也沒有向你炫耀的機會了。我們只有一張照片，那張照片現在擺在我面前。我表情很不好看，對著你放在我肩上的手翻著白眼。你笑得很開心，拉著我的手，還勸我多照幾張。
"There will be plenty of chances," I said. "The days are long." 「有機會的，日子長著呢。」我那樣說了。
Now, when I close my eyes, I still see you standing at the ship's rail, waving at me each time you came. You took the earliest boat on Saturday and the latest boat back on Sunday. And I would stay in Plymouth from Friday night until Monday morning. 如今我閉上眼睛，眼前還是你每次來看我時，站在船舷上向我招手的影子。你坐週六最早的船來，週日最晚的船回去。而我週五晚上就在普利茅斯住下，一直待到週一早晨。
Then war came. We lost contact for nearly five years. That afternoon, it had just stopped raining. The weather was glorious—sunlight bright, the air fresh. Roses were blooming wildly at the foot of the neighbour's house. On the velvet-green lawn across the street, a little girl in a sky-blue dress was playing with a tennis ball. A dog with a pink nose ran over, wagging its tail, retrieved the ball, placed it at her feet, and looked up with wide, shining black eyes, waiting for a pat on the head. I was trimming the holly in my garden. That shrub grows like mad at the start of summer. The television in the living room was on. News kept coming in about the German army seizing one place after another. As long as the bayonet is not pressed against our own throats, we always believe there is a wall between us and it—but there is only a sheet of paper. 後來戰爭開始了，我們幾乎斷了聯繫。那個午後剛下過雨，天氣好極了，陽光明媚，空氣清新，鄰居家的屋腳下玫瑰在怒放；對街綠絲絨般的草坪上，一個穿天藍色裙子的小女孩丟著網球，長著粉鼻子的狗搖著尾巴跑過去，把球叼回來，擺在主人面前，睜大又黑又亮的眼睛，等待腦袋上的一次撫摩。我正在修剪庭院裡的冬青，這種灌木一到初夏就開始瘋長。客廳裡電視機開著，不斷有新聞傳來，說德軍又佔領了哪裡。當刺刀沒有抵在我們脖子上時，我們永遠以為它跟我們隔著一堵牆，但只隔了一張紙。
Then came a deafening blast. I watched as the little girl's hand never reached the dog's head. Then—silence. A few seconds passed. It shouldn't have been tens of seconds—at least, perhaps it was minutes. From the surrounding houses came the cries of infants; women, their voices trembling, hummed formless lullabies; dogs barked at everything; the little girl across the street was carried inside by her mother, the tennis ball rolling into a gutter. The men were the slowest to react. A full five minutes later, they ran out in their house clothes, furious, determined to carry on with their lives as if nothing had happened. 一聲巨響後，我眼見著那個女孩的手沒能放在狗的腦袋上。接著是一片死寂。過了幾秒，不應該有幾十秒，至少，也可能有幾分鐘，四周的房子裡傳來半大嬰兒的哭聲；婦女們急急地用顫抖的聲音哼唱不成調的搖籃曲；狗對著任何東西亂吠；對街的小女孩被母親抱進房子，網球滾落在陰溝裡；男人們反應最為遲鈍，足足過了五分多鐘，他們才穿著家居服跑出來，怒氣沖天，固執地決定他們要繼續本来的生活。
It was a curious afternoon. Some citizens became hysterical, digging holes in their own immaculate lawns. Others tried to remain cheerful, draping an arm around a neighbour's shoulder, their pale faces forcing an incongruous smile. Some hid behind their curtains, scribbling frantically. That afternoon, many gunshots were heard—not from the enemy's dark weapons, but from our own shuddering hearts. That evening, the bodies of the suicides were carried out of their homes. Everyone prayed. And that same night, I received your letter: 那是個有趣的下午，一些市民歇斯底里，開始在自己華麗的草坪上挖洞；一些試圖保持愉快，把手搭上身邊人的肩膀，蒼白的臉上展示出一個不協調的微笑；有的躲在窗簾布後面，急匆匆地寫著什麼東西；那天下午傳來了不少槍聲，不是來自於敵人黑洞洞的武器，而來自於我們戰慄不已的心臟。晚上，人們把自殺者的屍體抬出房子，所有人都在祈禱。也是在那個晚上，我收到了你的信：
"I'm going to the front," you said. 「我上前線去了。」你說。
I joined the fire brigade. From the air raids on Croydon and Wimbledon onwards, the city became an unfamiliar territory. Meadows reverted to wasteland; the weeds along the roadsides gradually became the roads themselves; granite burned to a brown ochre. Most of the churches were destroyed, and fragments of cherub heads lay on the ground. Most of the cross shards were taken away; some believed they could offer some measure of protection. Residential areas crumbled into piles of bricks, with scraps of underwear hanging from blackened, bare walls. War created hundreds of thousands, perhaps millions, of orphans. We emptied the cargo ships that had carried steel to transport them to relatively safe countryside. Perhaps I saw too much greyish-yellow in those days, which is why everything I see now still seems thin and drained. 我加入了消防隊。從克洛伊敦和溫布頓的空襲開始，這座城市已經變成了陌生的領域，草地退回了荒地；道路兩旁的雜草逐漸演變成雜草中的道路；花崗岩燒成棕赭色；大多數的教堂都被毀了，地上摔著小天使頭部的碎片；十字架的碎片大多數被撿走了，有的人們相信它們可以帶來某種程度的庇佑；居民區退化成一堆磚頭，燻黑的裸牆上懸掛著內衣碎片；戰爭製造了幾十萬也可能有幾百萬的孤兒，我們空出運鋼鐵的貨船以把他們安全轉移到相對安全的鄉下。可能那時見了太多灰黃色，以至於現在看東西也總是單薄枯竭的了。
I don't like to recall the war. We had no contact for five long years. Letters, even if written, could never have been delivered—most of the house numbers had crumbled to dust. The sky hummed, as if giant insects were circling overhead. The winter was brutally cold; coal and potatoes were rationed as tightly as the corsets of women in centuries past. We survived, exhausted, hiding like woodlice under rocks. I busied myself rebuilding the city as it was being destroyed—because the moment I stopped, I could not help thinking: 'He's not coming back, is he.' 我不願回憶戰爭，我們沒有聯繫，長達五年。信就算寫了也不可能送到，因為大多數門牌都已經碎在了地上；天空嗡嗡響，像是有巨大的飛行昆蟲在頭頂上盤旋；冬天異常冷，煤炭和土豆的供應收得像前幾世紀婦女的束腰；我們疲倦地生存，像躲在角落石塊下的鼠婦；我積極地在城市被毀壞的同時重建它，因為一閒下來我就會控制不住地想「你不會回來了吧」。
Then, as quickly as it had begun, it was suddenly over. When we cherish the turning of the clock's hands, time seems to move faster for us too. The sky cleared. Celebrations followed the usual routine: decorations, parades, cheers. We had become shabby and tattered, having aged rapidly in those five years—just like the city itself. 就像開始一樣快，突然之間，一切都結束了。當我們努力珍惜指針的轉動時，對我們來說時間也會變快。天空晴朗了，慶祝儀式按照慣例張燈結彩，遊行歡呼，我們變得寒酸襤褸，在五年之間蒼老得迅速，和這座城市一樣。
I took the early morning train to Plymouth and rented a small room on the second floor near the harbour. Every day, the smell of fish was delivered unadorned by the sea wind. It was several months before the shipping lanes were restored. From the moment the first whistle sounded, I stood at the dock, dressed in the neatest clothes I could find. A child once asked me what I was doing. I said I was watching the seagulls. Then he gave me a handful of grain, telling me that this way the seagulls would land on my hand. 我凌晨坐車去了普利茅斯，在港口邊上租了一個二樓的小房間，每天都有魚腥味被海風不加修飾地送達。通航幾個月後才恢復，從第一聲汽笛響起，我就站在碼頭，穿著我能找到的最工整的衣服。有一個小孩曾問我在幹什麼，我說我在看海鷗，然後他塞給我了一把米，告訴我這樣海鷗會落在我的手上。
I came across some verses written before the war that I had never read before: 我讀到了一些戰前沒有讀到過的詩句：
But you must wait,
And do not join the others raising their glasses.
Wait for me, and I will come back.
Death, time and again, I shall defeat.
Let those who never waited call me lucky, be surprised. 你可要等下去
千萬不要同他們一起忙著舉起酒盞
等著我吧，我會回來
死神一次次被我挫敗
就讓那不曾等待我的人說我僥倖，感到意外
After that, I never attended any gatherings of friends. These days, most young women wear brightly coloured dresses—mostly red or yellow, velvet, with gold-threaded dark patterns on the hems beneath which their slender legs show. Their noses are flushed like ripe strawberries even when they haven't been drinking. Their highlighter sparkles like diamonds. The young men wear thin dark green wool suits woven with pale cream stripes, their burgundy-red ties neatly tucked into their jackets, fastened with goldfinch tie clips, almost without a single wrinkle. Their hair is oiled with rose oil, combed back, giving them a mature air. On their shoulders lean pretty young women. They talk about war as if talking about the weather, the same monotonous breakfast, or an entirely neutral football match—utterly ordinary. 我後來從未參加過任何朋友的聚會。現在女孩大多穿色澤明豔的裙子，紅黃居多，絲絨質地，繡著金絲暗紋的裙擺底下露出一雙細腿，哪怕沒有喝酒鼻尖也像熟草莓似的發紅，高光刷得亮亮晶晶，鑽石似的閃光；男人穿深綠薄呢西裝，編織進奶白細條紋，勃艮第紅酒顏色的領帶整整齊齊地塞進西裝外套裡，夾金絲雀領帶夾，幾乎看不出一絲皺褶。頭髮抹了玫瑰油，向後梳起，顯得老成；他們肩上靠著年輕漂亮的女郎，他們談論戰爭就像談論天氣，千篇一律的早飯，或是一場絕對中立的球賽一樣稀鬆平常。
I waited for ten years. Only when the list of the fallen was placed before me did I return to London. 我站了十年。直到犧牲者名單拍在我面前時我才回到倫敦去。
I found an ordinary clerical job at a newspaper. The advantage of this job was that I could see the newspaper early, and thus see in advance whether there was any missing persons information on that day's front page. I moved back to my old street, to a newly renovated two-storey house. Later, my wife moved in too. The little girl across the street was now the mother of two children. They had a new dog—it could not only fetch but also jump through hoops. 我在一家報社找了個普普通通的文書工作，這份工作的好處就是可以提前看到報紙，也就可以提前看到當天的版面上有沒有什麼尋人信息。我搬回了原來的街道，一棟粉飾一新的二層洋樓，後來我的妻子也搬了進來。我對街的女孩已經是兩個孩子的母親，他們有了一條新的狗，除了撿球還會跳圈。
I used to imagine the various ways you might return: if you had lost a leg, you would say 'at least it's not an arm, so I can still embrace you'; if you had lost an arm, you would say 'at least it's not a leg, so I can still dance with you'; if your body was broken and incomplete, you would say 'at least my handsome face is still here, so kissing you is still easy.' 我曾經想過你回來時可能的面貌：如果你缺了腿，你會說幸好不是胳膊，這樣還能擁抱我；如果你缺了胳膊，你會說幸好不是腿，你還能與我共舞；如果你的軀體殘缺不全，你會說幸好你英俊的面容尚存，親吻對你來說依舊易如反掌。
To all my family and friends, I said I had already forgotten you. 所有親友提起時我都說我已經把你忘了。
I am almost at the station. This is the last time I will come here. My body has aged much faster than I imagined. This morning, I had to hold onto the bedside table for a long while just to sit up. It takes me nearly an hour to get dressed. When I was young, I only spent that long getting ready before parties—but that was because of careful selection. I forget many things. When I make those scones you used to mock, I forget to add the other ingredients. 我快到站了，這是我最後一次來這裡了，我的軀幹老得比我想像得快上許多，今天早上我已經要扶著床頭櫃撐好一會才能坐起來了。我穿好一套衣服要將近一個小時，年輕時我只有聚會前才會花那麼久時間，只不過那是因為精挑細選。我記不得很多事，當我做被你嘲笑的司康餅時我會忘記放輔料。
Time is so cold. Time is so warm. I will not have to endure many more days and nights before I see you again. 時間太冷漠了，時間太溫暖了，我不用熬過多少個日夜就能再見到你了。
I am old, Francis. I am seventy-eight. 我老了，弗朗西斯，我已經七十八歲了。
After the old man had hobbled away, I folded the letter and put it in my pocket. As I stepped out of the railway station, it began to snow—snow as faint as dust. Breathing the sooty air, I felt as if I were inhaling a frozen soul. Then I had three days of leave. I crossed the English Channel by boat, and the ship docked at the port of Brest. I took a walk along the seashore. The place was beautiful, and the air seemed to carry the scent of lemon verbena. At four in the morning, I went down to the sea. The water was still dark; I could hear the echoes of the reefs. By the sea, I burned the letter and scattered its reddish ashes into the cold water. When I returned to the inn, dawn was breaking again. In the open space before the inn stood several rosemary bushes, which seemed to have sprouted white buds overnight. Perhaps they had always been there—only I had never noticed them before. 老人蹣跚離去後，我把信折疊好裝進口袋，走出火車站的時候下雪了，雪花像塵埃般微茫。呼吸著煙塵般的空氣，我像是在把冰冷的靈魂吸進肺裡。接著我有了三天的休假，我坐船渡過英吉利海峽，輪船停泊在布雷斯特港。我在海邊散了散步，這地方景色宜人，空氣似乎也散發著檸檬草的香氣。我在凌晨四點時去了海邊，海面還是暗色的，能聽見礁石的回音。我在海邊燒掉了那封信，把它發紅的灰燼撒進冰冷的海水。我回旅館時清晨再次來臨，旅館門前的空地上栽了數株迷迭香，好像一夜之間長出了白色骨朵。它們也許一直在那兒，但我以前卻注意不到。
