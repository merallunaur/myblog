A Clash of Eras: An Interview with Mr Arthur Kirkland 兩段時光的紛爭：與亞瑟·柯克蘭先生的一次訪談

"Renowned entrepreneur and philanthropist, Mr. Arthur Kirkland, now sixty-eight years old, recently auctioned his late lover—the famous painter Mr. Francis Bonnefoy's—posthumous work titled 'Called Forgetting' at the Nago Auction House in Verona. Bidders offered extremely high prices, but in the end, Mr. Kirkland personally participated in the bidding and bought the painting back for a record price of 5.08 million US dollars. We are equally curious about this move. We are extremely grateful to Mr. Kirkland for accepting an interview with our newspaper and generously agreeing to answer any questions." 「著名企業家、慈善家，現已六十八歲的亞瑟·柯克蘭先生，前幾日在維羅納的納高拍賣行拍賣自己已故愛人，著名畫家弗朗西斯·波諾弗瓦先生的遺作《名為遺忘》。各買家出價極高，而最後柯克蘭先生卻親自參與競價，以508萬美金的天價又買回了這幅畫。對於此舉，我們同樣好奇。我們萬分感謝柯克蘭先生，受本報採訪，並且慷慨地接受任何問題。」
[Opening: Goodnight, Kitten] 【開場：小貓晚安】
M: Hello, Mr. Kirkland. I've long admired you. M： 您好，柯克蘭先生，久仰。
Arthur: Hello. Likewise. 亞瑟： 您好，久仰。
M: First of all, thank you for the tremendous contributions your foundation, "Goodnight, Kitten," has made to cancer research. To be honest, the name surprised me—it doesn't quite match your usual serious and cautious demeanor. M： 首先感謝您的基金會，小貓晚安，對癌症的研究做出的巨大貢獻。坦白來說，這名字讓我大吃一驚，畢竟與您平時嚴肅，謹慎的風度不大相符。
Arthur: That was actually Francis's idea. That damn—oh, sorry. I've been compromising with him my entire life. 亞瑟： 這其實是弗朗西斯的意思。那個混……哦，抱歉。我一生都在向他妥協。
[Time: Forty-Six Years] 【時間：四十六年】
M: How long were you together? M： 您和他在一起多長時間？
Arthur: Forty-six years. 亞瑟： 四十六年。
M: No offense, but I recall that Mr. Bonnefoy passed away in 1980. That was thirty-nine years ago. He was only thirty-two. M： 無意冒犯，我記得波諾弗瓦先生於1980年去世，那是三十九年前，他不過三十二歲。
Arthur: That's correct. I met him in 1973. From then until now, no more, no less—forty-six years. 亞瑟： 沒錯。我遇到他時是1973年，到現在，不多不少，四十六年。
[Memory: Searching for Stars in the Midday Sky] 【記憶：晌午碧空找星星】
M: Do you still remember things from forty-six years ago? Or even earlier? M： 您還記得四十六年前的事情嗎，或許更早？
Arthur: A small part. It's gradually becoming blurry. Sometimes I hate myself for not having Francis's talent—those things on paper, on canvas, the flowing ink, the bright colors. With a single stroke, they are almost fixed in form. Take the first portrait he painted of me, which he titled 'Adonis'—the beautiful youth of Greek mythology, the plant god, forever young. The portrait he painted of me has indeed never changed: emerald green, gold dust, pinkish-orange, bluish-black—as vivid as decades ago. But my face has grown many wrinkles. Francis's original intention was to use the canvas to capture my time, yet I have aged in its place. Human memory is shaped by time into this form or that, or else erased entirely—deceiving oneself into thinking that what has been forgotten was nothing but weeds beneath the flowers or mud on the soles of one's shoes. Recalling these things is as difficult as searching for stars in the midday sky. Even if I force myself to review them in my mind every day, my memory no longer allows me to live in the past. 亞瑟： 一小部分吧，已經逐漸模糊了。有時候我會恨自己沒有弗朗西斯一樣的才華，那些紙的東西，布的東西，淋漓的筆墨，明豔的色彩，一筆下去，幾乎就定了型。就像弗朗西斯給我畫的第一張肖像，他取名叫《阿多尼斯》，希臘神話裡的美男子，植物神，年輕容顏不老。他給我畫的肖像確實從沒變過，祖母綠、砂金、橘粉、發藍的漆黑，像幾十年前一樣鮮亮，我的臉上倒是長出好幾道皺紋，弗朗西斯畫這幅畫的本意是用畫紙把我的時光留住，我卻替它老了。人的記憶被時間塑成這種模樣，那種造型，或者乾脆全都抹去，欺騙自己說忘掉的都是花下雜草，鞋底爛泥。回憶這些事情就像在晌午的碧空裡找星星一樣困難。就算我每天都強迫自己在腦子裡複習一遍，我的記憶也已經不允許我活在過去了。
[Longing: As Ordinary as Breathing] 【想念：像呼吸一樣平常】
M: It seems you still think of him often. M： 看來您還會常常想起他。
Arthur: That man would be very happy to hear you say that (laughs). Decades have passed. Perhaps I have already almost forgotten him. Perhaps thinking of him has become as ordinary to me as breathing, drinking tea, or eating dinner—so I no longer pay attention to it. 亞瑟： 那傢伙聽您這樣說會很高興的（笑）。過去幾十年了，也許我已經快把他忘了，也許想起他對我來說已經像呼吸、喝茶、吃晚飯一樣平平無奇，所以我不再注意了。
[Definition: From "Frog" to "Poetry"] 【定義：從「青蛙」到「詩篇」】
M: To you, what kind of person was Mr. Bonnefoy? Almost all art journals, newspapers, and magazines print a three-inch photo next to his works, with a small line underneath reading 'Francis Bonnefoy, renowned painter'—but nothing more. This has rendered Mr. Bonnefoy pale and flat in the eyes of most people. M： 對您來說，波諾弗瓦先生是個怎樣的人？幾乎所有藝術期刊，報紙，雜誌，都會在他的作品左側印上一张三寸照片，底下一行小字注「弗朗西斯·波諾弗瓦，著名畫家」，但也只有這麼多，這使得波諾弗瓦先生在大多數人面前都蒼白了，平面了。
Arthur: Francis is by no means pale or flat. He is Monet's palette, Van Gogh's brush-rinsing pot, Baudelaire's poetry, Mozart's乐章. He is... hmm... 亞瑟： 弗朗西斯絕對不是蒼白而平面的。他是莫內的調色盤、梵谷的洗筆池、波特萊爾的詩篇、莫札特的華章。他，嗯……
M: This question has given you pause. M： 這個問題引起了您的深思。
Arthur: Indeed. If you had asked me this question more than forty years ago, I would have answered without hesitation: 'lazybones,' 'narcissist,' 'French frog'—which might not sound very polite (laughs). When we bickered, I never held back. 亞瑟： 的確。如果你在四十多年前問起這個問題，我一定會不假思索地回答「懶散鬼」「自戀狂」「法國青蛙」，可能聽起來不太文雅（笑）。和他拌嘴的時候我從不嘴下留情。
M: It seems you don't think that way anymore. M： 看來您現在不這麼想了。
Arthur: That's not quite right. Francis's face—in my memory, it is still the same face, a young and handsome face unweathered by dust. So when I think of him, I still involuntarily reach for those worn-out words. He was tall and lean, loved to wear long light-colored shirts and dark trench coats, dressing himself like a black-feathered swallow. His eyes were selfish and domineering, claiming an entire morning's iris field for themselves, often shimmering with the dew on blue-purple petals. When he had no particular emotion, he would smile—neither hot nor cold. He fit the stereotypical Frenchman perfectly: arrogant, proud, romantic, and amorous. He became acquainted with me over a proffered mojito and, unexpectedly, lived with me for seven years. My initial definition of this relationship was 'a three-month collision'—like the first snow of winter, brief and impatient, fleeing in haste before its charm had faded. 亞瑟： 並非如此。弗朗西斯的臉，在我的記憶裡還是同一張臉，一張沒有被塵埃磨蝕的，年輕英俊的臉，所以我想到他時還是會不由自主地想到這些被我用爛的詞。他身材頎長、瘦削，愛穿長淺色襯衫和深色風衣外套，把自己拾掇得像一隻黑羽毛的燕子。眼睛自私霸道，獨佔了一整片清晨的鳶尾花田，時常閃爍著藍紫花瓣上露水的光亮。沒有特別情感時他就笑著，不慍不火。他很符合模子裡刻出來的法國人形象，高傲自大，浪漫多情。他靠著一杯遞過來的莫吉托跟我熟絡起來，並且意料之外的跟我生活了七年。我最初對這段感情的定義是「三個月的碰撞」，像冬日落的第一場雪，短促又急躁，興味未盡就匆忙逃離。
[Core: The Clarity of a Goshawk] 【內核：鷂鷹般的清醒】
M: He sounds like a truly charming person. M： 聽起來他實在是個很有魅力的人。
Arthur: Within his heart burned a passion hotter than fire—fearless and unyielding. The era, blessed with a genetic disposition toward historical optimism, always shows us its radiant rosy face, its upturned laughing eyes, its green velvet dress, its black star-diamond necklace, its ruby and sapphire rings with gold settings on fair slender fingers. It always presents only one side to the world, while its exposed back is covered in festering sores and blood scabs—ugly enough to make one retch—only hidden by silk, chiffon, and merino wool. Francis was not foolishly optimistic. He was not a sparrow fluttering in a net amid London's hazy smog or Paris's dreamscape of gold leaf. He was as clear-sighted as a goshawk—from the waking eye of dawn to the unknitted brows of the moon goddess—and as awake as if bathing in the cold waters of the Arctic Ocean. 亞瑟： 他的心中有一種，比火更加灼熱的情感，無畏、無妄。時代天生歷史樂觀基因，總把她容光煥發的玫瑰色面龐，上挑的笑眼，碧色絲絨衣裙，黑星鑽項鍊，白皙纖手上紅寶石，藍寶石的鑲金底座戒指展示給我們看。永遠以一面示人，露出的背脊上卻爬滿膿瘡，血痂，醜陋得令人作嘔，只是平常被絲綢、雪紡、美利奴遮住了。弗朗西斯並非愚蠢樂觀，他不是在倫敦的朦朧煙幕，或巴黎的金紙夢境中，在羅網中撲騰的一隻麻雀。他如鷂鷹般曉暢，從黎明醒眼，到月神展眉，他像在北冰洋的冷水裡一樣清醒。
M: Those who can tear off the mask of the era without falling into despair are great people. M： 在撕下時代的面具後還不陷入絕望的人，是偉大的人。
Arthur: It seems I am dissecting his corpse. 亞瑟： 我似乎正在解剖他的屍體。
[Diagnosis: The Truth of the Snowy Night in Prague] 【診斷：布拉格雪夜的真相】
M: When did you learn that Mr. Bonnefoy was ill? M： 您是何时得知波諾弗瓦先生患病的？
Arthur: December 10, 1978. We were in Prague then, and Christmas was half a month away. You must have been to Prague—or at least seen photographs. The night sky was ever-changing: the dome was dark blue, and near the horizon it blended into purple, the two colors merging without a clear boundary, like paint dripping into water—very much like someone's eyes. It was snowing, and a layer had settled on the cobblestone streets. Francis insisted on not stepping on it, so we stood by the roadside, surrounded by houses glowing with warm yellow light. By day those houses looked like gingerbread; by night they were drizzled with dark chocolate syrup. The snow fell heavily, dusting them with a layer of powdered sugar. 亞瑟： 1978年12月10日，那時我們在布拉格，半個月之後就是聖誕節。您應該去過布拉格，至少也應該看過照片。夜空變幻莫測，頂穹是深藍色，邊緣接著地平線的地方是紫色，兩種顏色沒有清晰界線，像滴在水裡的顏料一樣融在一起，很像某人的眼睛。正在下雪，方磚路上鋪了一層，弗朗西斯執意不踩上去，於是我們在路沿站著，周圍都是亮著昏黃燈光的房子。那些房子白天看像薑餅做的，晚上就淋一層黑巧克力漿，雪下得很大，又撒上一層糖霜。
M: Your memory is remarkably clear. Mr. Bonnefoy's work 'Letters from Prague' must have been created around that time. It seems you learned of this in a rather romantic setting. M： 您的記憶真是清晰。想必波諾弗瓦先生的作品《布拉格的信件》就是那時創作的吧。看來您是在一個相當浪漫的場景裡知道這件事的。
Arthur: Yes. The light was very dim. The winter night enveloped us. Someone was playing the saxophone—the tone was languid and lingering, thread by thread—'Casablanca.' Francis lit a cigarette, took only a few puffs, then scratched his head until it was fluffy and messy, and stubbed the cigarette out in the snow. After that little orange-red spark went dark, he took the diagnosis from his pocket and handed it to me. 亞瑟： 是。光線很暗，冬夜籠罩了我們，有人在吹薩克斯風，音色慵懶繾綣，絲絲縷縷，是《卡薩布蘭卡》。弗朗西斯點著一根煙，沒吸幾口又撓著頭，撓得又蓬又亂，然後把煙按在雪地上。當那顆小小的橘紅火星暗下去之後他從口袋裡把診斷書掏給了我。
M: He didn't go to the hospital with you? M： 他沒有和您一起去醫院？
Arthur: No. At the time, I was running our gallery, selling his painting of wilting irises—he had imitated Van Gogh's style, and many people liked it. That painting was called 'Immortality.' He went to the hospital alone, probably around mid-November. He had started fainting frequently a few months earlier, but because he had always suffered from low blood sugar—he was as thin as an iris stem—I would always stuff a piece of hard fruit candy into his mouth each time. But during that month between his knowing and his telling me, he was still the same as before: the curve of his smile, his daily attire, his goodbye kiss before leaving home, his lark-like tone in his morning and evening greetings—pathetically, nothing had changed. 亞瑟： 沒有。我那時經營著我們的畫廊，出售他的那幅枯萎鳶尾的畫，他模仿了梵谷的風格，很多人喜歡。那幅畫叫《永生》。他自己偷偷去了醫院，應該是11月中旬的事情，他之前幾個月開始頻繁暈倒，但因為他一直有低血糖的毛病，他瘦得像鳶尾的莖，所以每次我都會往他嘴裡塞一塊水果硬糖。但是，從他自己知道到他告訴我的這一個月間，他還和以前一樣，他微笑的弧度，平日的裝束，離家前的告別吻，早安和晚安那百靈鳥一樣的語氣，可悲的從未改變。
[Mark: The Taste of Rust and the Iris on His Lower Back] 【印記：鐵鏽味與後腰的鳶尾】
M: Mr. Bonnefoy was a gentle person. Perhaps he was looking for a way for you to accept it, or perhaps he himself was trying to accept it. M： 波諾弗瓦先生是一個溫柔的人。或許他在找讓您接受的辦法，或許他自己也在試著接受。
Arthur: Our personalities diverged or even opposed each other in many ways. I am stubborn, violent, and extreme. So the first thing I did after reading the diagnosis was to grab his shoulders and punch his arm. 亞瑟： 他與我性格有很多地方相離甚至相反。我偏執、暴力、過激，所以我看清診斷書後的第一件事，就是抓住他的肩膀往他胳膊上打了一拳。
M: That seems rather cruel for a sick person. M： 對於一個病人這似乎有些殘忍。
Arthur: Hmm (laughs). I also said a lot of nonsense to him—more illogical and irrational words than I've ever said even when drunk on tequila. It was as if I were the one who had gone mad. I bit his lip like a wild animal. I still remember the taste of rust that burst and exploded on my tongue—it reminded me that he still existed. At that moment, only one thought was in my mind: I had to brand his mark onto my body. Later, I went to a tattoo parlor and had a iris flower engraved on my lower back. 亞瑟： 嗯（笑），我還跟他說了很多胡話，我喝龍舌蘭喝醉的時候都沒有說過那麼多毫無邏輯和理性可言的話，就好像我才是病瘋了的那個。我像隻野獸一樣咬他的唇瓣，我還記得在舌面上綻開，爆炸的鐵鏽味，這提醒了我他還存在。當時我腦子裡只有一個念頭：我必須讓他的印記烙在我身上。後來我去紋身店在後腰刻了一朵香根鳶尾。
M: On your lower back? Usually people tattoo it on their chest—you know, close to the heart. M： 腰上？似乎一般都會紋在胸口，您知道，靠近心臟的位置。
Arthur: I felt there was no more room to engrave there. 亞瑟： 我覺得那裡已經刻不下了。
M: How did Mr. Bonnefoy react? M： 波諾弗瓦先生有什麼反應嗎？
Arthur: He held me, wrapped his coat around me because I was trembling all over. He stroked my hair, called my name softly, and told me he was not afraid of the illness—no one could escape it; everyone was its captive. 亞瑟： 他抱著我，張開外套裹在我身上，因為我渾身發抖。摸我的頭髮，輕聲叫我的名字，跟我說他不害怕疾病，沒人能逃脫得了，所有人都是它的俘虜。
M: Perhaps he didn't say it, but the only thing he feared was your loneliness. M： 可能他沒說，他唯一害怕的就是您的孤獨。
Arthur: I don't like to speculate about his thoughts. I've never guessed correctly. 亞瑟： 我不喜歡去揣測他的想法，我從未猜對過。
[During Illness: Days of Pain and Days of Silence] 【病中：疼與不喊疼的日子】
M: How did Mr. Bonnefoy behave after falling ill? M： 波諾弗瓦先生患病後有什麼表現嗎？
Arthur: I didn't wait until Christmas; I forced us to fly back to London. I remember that night in Prague he was squatting on the ground, his eyes nailed to the wooden plank of the sky, blinking along with the stars. The night clung to him. I pulled him up and said, 'Get up, Fran. You have to get treatment. I still need you to make money.' 亞瑟： 我沒等到聖誕節就強迫我們飛回倫敦，我記得布拉格的那天晚上他蹲在地上，雙眼像鐵釘一樣釘上天空的木板，跟著滿天繁星一齊眨眼。黑夜黏在他身上。我把他從地上拉起來，跟他說「站起來，弗朗吉，你必須給我好好治病，我還要你賺錢」。
M: 'I still need you to make money'? Wouldn't that have hurt Mr. Bonnefoy? M： 「我還要你賺錢」？波諾弗瓦先生會很難過吧。
Arthur: He knew me, so he didn't. During chemotherapy, Francis always cried out in pain. He grew thinner day by day, though occasionally, when he had energy, he would paint. Those paintings had no signatures, no titles—fragmented and abstract. Sometimes he would slap down a red patch and then carefully paint pale blue vein-like lines over it. Other times he would sprinkle dark purple, emerald green, and thick gold pigments onto the paper, watching them shimmer like pearls in the sunlight. I still keep those paintings today, as part of my collection. 亞瑟： 他知道我，所以他不會。化療時弗朗西斯總是喊疼。他一天比一天消瘦下去，不過偶爾有精神的時候會畫個畫。那些畫沒有署名，沒有主題，瑣碎又抽象。有時他會甩上一個紅色的色塊，然後細細畫上淡藍葉脈般的紋路。有時他會把絳紫、祖母綠、濃金顏料灑在紙面上，看它們在太陽底下閃著珍珠似的光。那些畫我現在還留著，作為收藏。
M: Mr. Bonnefoy's endurance was extraordinary. M： 波諾弗瓦先生的忍耐力超乎尋常。
Arthur: He was in too much pain. I also felt he was unhappy. But when he proposed stopping treatment, I agreed. That was March 25, 1979—just as spring was arriving. 亞瑟： 他太疼了，我也覺得他不快樂。不過疾病本身就讓人無法綻開笑顏。但是他提出先停止治療時，我還是同意了，那是1979年3月25日，春天剛到。
[The Last Year: Breakfast Under a Black Curtain] 【最後一年：黑色幕布下的早餐】
M: What did you do during that year? M： 一年間你們做了什麼？
Arthur: Nothing much. At the end of the lifeline, you don't think about surging waves, thunderous storms, or spring streams. You might only think about whether to have pancakes or toast for breakfast tomorrow, whether to spread blueberry jam or salad dressing, whether to pair it with milk or black coffee. Illness drains your romantic core, strangles the tender branches and leaves, and shoots through the wings behind your back. That is illness: bland, tedious, chilling—an unpeelable black curtain, a gloomy sky that refuses to brighten, an oil lamp that won't light. But sometimes you can't help but consider that you might not even get to eat tomorrow's breakfast. 亞瑟： 沒什麼，在生命線的末端你不會想到什麼波翻雲湧、雷奔電激、花溪春水，你可能只會想明天早上吃鬆餅還是吐司，抹藍莓醬還是沙拉醬，配牛奶還是黑咖啡。疾病抽走你的浪漫內核，扼斷出枝的柔嫩花葉，射穿你背後的翅膀。這就是疾病，平淡、乏味、膽寒，一塊揭不開的黑色幕布，一片亮不起的陰沉天空，一盞點不亮的油燈。但有時候你會不自主地考慮到，你可能連明天的早餐都吃不上了。
[Deterioration: Praying to and Cursing the Gods] 【惡化：向神明祈禱與辱罵】
M: The following year, Mr. Bonnefoy's condition worsened. M： 第二年波諾弗瓦先生的病情惡化了。
Arthur: June 8. When he was loaded into the ambulance, I was still at the gallery organizing his paintings. Dozens had already been sold, but there were still many. I put fresh flowers in the vase by the gallery door, cleaned up, locked the door, and then received the call. When I arrived at the hospital room, he was unconscious. The white sheet covering him made his face look even paler—like a porcelain doll in a music box. I walked over, knelt down, and took his hand. His hand was hard, his knuckles prominent, as cold as snow. I knelt there, cursing every god I knew, then praying to them—but we no longer had the gods' protection. 亞瑟： 6月8日。他被抬上救護車的時候我還在畫廊整理他的畫，那些畫已經賣掉好幾十張，但還有不少。我給畫廊門邊的花瓶換上新的鮮花，打掃乾淨，鎖上門，接到了電話。當我趕到病房時他正昏迷，白色被單蓋在他身上，襯得臉色也愈發蒼白，像個八音盒裡的瓷娃娃一樣。我走過去，跪下來，握住他的手。他的手很硬，骨節分明，雪一般涼。我跪在那裡，辱罵了我所知道的所有神明後又向他們祈禱，但我們已經得不到神明的庇佑。
M: You didn't describe his expression. Have you forgotten it? M： 您好像沒有描述他的表情，您記不清了嗎？
Arthur: I was afraid to see his bloodless lips, just as I never tended the hyacinth patch outside our house. Later, Francis woke up and began treatment again. He never cried out in pain anymore. I entrusted the gallery to someone else, turned away the reporters. I lived in the hospital—not to make him grateful to me, but to let him know that someone else was walking toward death alongside him. 亞瑟： 我害怕看見毫無血色的嘴唇，就像我從不打理我們屋外的風信子花叢一樣。後來弗朗西斯醒了過來，開始接受治療，他再也不喊疼了。我把畫廊託給別人管理，記者趕走。我住在醫院裡，不是讓他對我心存感激，而是讓他知道有人和他一樣，正向著死亡邁步。
[Birth: The Memory Balls of 'Called Forgetting'] 【誕生：《名為遺忘》的記憶球】
M: When did Mr. Bonnefoy complete 'Called Forgetting'? To be honest, one would not imagine that painting came from the hand of a dying man. It depicts several small hill-like piles of colorful round balls—like a child's scribbles, with extremely high saturation—but some of the balls are gray, black, or white, tossed at the foot of the hills. In the foreground is only a black silhouette. It is as if a rainbow-colored paint bottle has been torn open in the sky; the canvas is radiant and dazzling. Though the colors occasionally jump, the overall effect is more serene than a symphony or a concerto. M： 波諾弗瓦先生是什麼時候完成那幅《名為遺忘》的？實話說，那幅畫想像不到出自一個瀕死之人之手。畫上是幾堆小山一樣花花綠綠的圓球，像小孩子的信筆塗鴉，飽和度極高，但是有部分圓球是灰的、黑的、白的，扔在小山的腳下。畫面前方只有一個黑色剪影。撕裂了天空的彩虹顏料瓶，畫紙上流光溢彩，雖然顏色偶有跳躍，但比一支交響樂，或協奏曲，要更平和。
Arthur: It was a morning in mid-July. The weather was fine. Francis and I went out for a walk and fed some sparrows in the hospital courtyard. There was a citrus tree in the courtyard that didn't bear oranges, but its leaves were green and glossy, each one shining as if waxed. Along the gravel path, jasmine, carnations, and small rain lilies were in full bloom, their fragrance overwhelming, interspersed with dewy green leaves. The sun hung above the hospital spire, round and red like a salted egg yolk, its grease seeping into the white clouds at the edges, dyeing them orange. The path was covered in shadows; Francis's face flickered between light and dark, giving his smile a bittersweet expression. There were other patients' children, about five or six years old—still at an age when they knew nothing. They wore floral dresses and neat little suits, their hair braided and flying as they ran—full of vitality. 亞瑟： 那是7月中旬早晨，風和日麗，我和弗朗西斯出去走了走，給醫院裡的麻雀餵了點穀子。醫院裡有棵柑橘樹，不結橘子，但葉片綠油油的，每一片都像打了蠟似的閃著光。石子路兩邊素馨花、石竹花、小葱蘭怒放，花香撲鼻，中間夾著些濕漉漉的綠葉。太陽掛在醫院尖頂上，鹹蛋黃一樣又圓又紅，油漬滲入邊緣的白雲裡，白雲也染成橘紅色。路上佈滿陰影，弗朗西斯的臉忽明忽暗，顯得他的微笑悲欣交集。有其他病人的孩子，差不多五六歲，還什麼都不知道的年紀。穿著花裙子和乖巧的小西裝，頭髮束成辮子，隨著跑動飛舞，生氣勃勃。
M: You mean that scene moved him? M： 您是說那個場景觸動了他？
Arthur: Perhaps. Who can know an artist's heart? After we returned, he kept looking in the mirror. The light reflected from the mirror fell into his eyes. His eyes—layer upon layer of deep purple covering light blue—were like diving to explore a pond or cutting open a purple gemstone: the deeper you went, the richer the color; the closer to the crystalline surface, the lighter it became. By then he was terribly thin, and because of the treatment, he had shaved off his proud golden hair. He touched his face with his pale fingertips while I watched over him. This went on for about five minutes. Then he turned and called me. He smiled and said he had now become such an ugly sight—even a skeleton was more handsome than him. He hadn't painted in a long time. He had nothing left worth remembering. 亞瑟： 也許吧，誰知道一個藝術家的心呢？我們回去後他不停照鏡子，那鏡子反出的光落入他眼底。他的眼睛，重重疊疊深紫蓋著淺藍，就像下潛去探索一個池塘，或切開一顆紫色寶石，越往下越濃，越接近水晶質地的表面越淡。他那時瘦得不成樣子，因為治療，引以為傲的金髮也剃掉了。他拿蒼白的指尖摸著自己的臉，我在旁邊監視他，大概持續了五分多鐘吧，他扭頭叫我。他笑著說他現在已經變成了這麼一副醜樣子，連骷髏都比他俊美，他已經很久沒作畫了，他再也沒有什麼值得人記住的東西了。
M: But the world has remembered him for decades—because of you. M： 可世界記了他幾十年，因為您的緣故。
Arthur: Yes. At the time, I was tongue-tied and couldn't even utter a word of comfort. But he didn't seem to expect any comfort from me. He sighed softly and told me to bring his painting supplies. 亞瑟： 對，當時我笨嘴拙舌，連一句安慰的話都說不出來，不過他似乎並沒有期盼我的安慰。他輕輕嘆了口氣，吩咐我把畫具拿來。
M: And then 'Called Forgetting' was born. M： 然後就誕生了《名為遺忘》。
Arthur: Yes. It took about a week. He handed me the painting and said to me: 'These are your memory balls. The red, orange, green, and blue ones are what you remember; the black, white, and gray ones are what you have forgotten.' 亞瑟： 是。大約花了一週，他把畫交給我，對我說這是我的記憶球，紅橙綠藍的是我記住的，黑白灰是我遺忘的。
M: The silhouette is him. M： 剪影是他。
Arthur: But he was more vivid than ever. 亞瑟： 但他比任何時候都更鮮豔。
[Auction: Showing Off and Belonging] 【拍賣：炫耀與歸屬】
M: So you auctioned this painting to make people remember him? M： 所以您拍賣這幅畫，是想讓人記住他？
Arthur: What Francis loved most was praise. But critics only interpret the painting through their own shallow and limited understanding, fabricating all sorts of bizarre life stories for him. The auction was my way of showing off. Don't say 'art belongs to the whole world'—art is broad and inclusive, but he belongs to me. We belong to each other. 亞瑟： 弗朗西斯最愛的就是讚美，但評論家們只會通過自己粗淺，菲薄的認識去解讀這幅畫，進而給他編造出各種奇形怪狀的身世故事。拍賣，是我的炫耀，不要說什麼「藝術是全世界的藝術」，藝術是廣博而包容的，但他屬於我，我們屬於彼此。
[Farewell: Quicksand and 'Goodnight, Kitten'] 【永別：流沙與「晚安小貓」】
M: You and he were true soulmates. Did you have a premonition when he passed? M： 您和他，是真正的靈魂伴侶。他離開時您有預感嗎？
Arthur: I was by his side. He succeeded in making me experience the most unpleasant experience of my life. 亞瑟： 我在他的身邊。他成功地讓我經歷了我一生中最不愉快的體驗。
M: I'm sorry. Making you recall this is cruel. M： 對不起，讓您回憶是一件殘忍的事。
Arthur: It's alright. As he left, he looked into my eyes. His face was ravaged. I could see the light in his eyes gradually fading, his breathing growing softer and shallower. We didn't speak—not a word. We held hands. I could feel something—like quicksand—slipping through my fingers. I didn't try to grasp it; it would have been futile. When the quicksand was about to disappear completely, he said to me, 'Goodnight, kitten.' I kissed that hand. Francis closed his eyes. His thick, tassel-like eyelashes covered those two dead cat's-eye gems, like black butterflies closing their wings. It was September 26, 1980—just as autumn was arriving. 亞瑟： 沒有關係。他離開的時候看著我的眼睛，他的面容備受摧殘，我能看到他眼中的光芒逐漸微弱下去，呼吸又輕又淺。我們不說話，什麼都不說，拉著手，我能感覺到什麼東西，流沙似的，從我指間流走，我沒有試著伸手去抓，那將是徒勞。當那流沙將要完全消逝時他對我說「晚安小貓」，我吻了吻那隻手。弗朗西斯闔上了眼，濃密流蘇般的睫毛蓋住了那兩顆死掉的貓眼石，像闔翅的黑色蝴蝶。那時是1980年9月26日，秋天剛來。
M: You held his funeral alone, without allowing anyone to pay their respects. M： 您一個人舉辦了他的葬禮，沒讓任何人來弔唁。
Arthur: Yes (laughs). So you see, I really am selfish. 亞瑟： 對（笑）。這樣看我還真是自私。
[Afterward: The Möbius Strip and a Youthful Posture] 【此後：莫比烏斯環與年輕姿態】
M: How did his departure affect you? M： 他的離去對您有什麼影響？
Arthur: Have you heard of a Möbius strip? 亞瑟： 您聽說過莫比烏斯環嗎？
M: That strip of paper that is twisted and glued together so it has only one side? M： 那種扭轉一頭，然後黏在一起，所以只有一面的紙帶？
Arthur: That is the state of my life now. Francis has trapped me in this predicament. He has become my sigh, my breath, my sunrise, my moonset, the creases in my trench coat, the scuffs on my leather shoes. If anything can be polished brighter by abrasion, besides a mirror, it is his face. 亞瑟： 這就是我現在的生活狀態，弗朗西斯讓我陷入窘境，他變成了我的嘆息、我的呼吸、日出、月落、風衣的皺褶、皮鞋上的擦痕。如果有什麼能被磨蝕得更加光亮，除了鏡子就是他的臉。
M: If he were standing in front of you right now, what would you do? M： 如果他現在站在您面前，您會？
Arthur: Hmm, if he were standing in front of me... ah, then he would still be a young man. I am thirty-six years older than him. He would surely mock my wrinkled face, my graying hair, my bark-like skin, and my cloudy, impure eyes. He would still be young, handsome, elegant, and romantic. If he mocked me, I would definitely hit him with my cane (laughs). He would no longer want to kiss this wretched old man he hasn't seen for years—not anymore. 亞瑟： 嗯，他站在我面前……啊，那他一定是個年輕人，我比他多老了三十六年，他一定會嘲笑我皺紋叢生的面龐，泛白的頭髮，樹皮一樣的皮膚和混濁的雜質雙眼。他還一樣年輕英俊瀟灑風流，如果他嘲笑我的話，我一定會拿手杖敲他的（笑）。他不會再想吻我這個久別重逢的糟老頭子了，不會了。
M: One last question: describe Mr. Bonnefoy in one sentence, so that the magazine won't print only 'renowned painter' next to his artwork. M： 最後一個問題，用一句話形容一下波諾弗瓦先生吧，好讓雜誌登畫時不只有著名畫家。
Arthur: The strife between two eras. 亞瑟： 兩段時光的紛爭。
"After that, we talked about current events and the weather for a while. Mr. Kirkland rose to leave. He put on his hat, covering his platinum-streaked hair. Wearing a well-fitted cobalt blue suit, he walked with a straight back. When his figure was swallowed by the crowd, I realized that he walked like a young man in his twenties." 「後來我們談了一些時事和天氣，柯克蘭先生起身告別。他戴上帽子，遮住了白金斑駁的頭髮，著合身的鈷藍西裝，走起路來腰背挺拔。當他的背影被人流攫取，我意識到他走路的樣子像一個二十多歲的小伙子。」
