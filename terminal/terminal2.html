Number 536 was fortunate. His great-great-grandfather was among the first scientists to send humans into space. His efforts benefited every generation thereafter. Class A citizen Number 536. His number was inherited from the purple star HD185198 named by his great-great-grandfather—also known as "Captain 536." Thus, he always had a strange premonition of connecting with past time and space, as if he himself were an heirloom left over from a bygone era. A ghostly prayer, sung from the mouth of a prophet. Especially 1985—the year his great-great-grandfather had been the same age as he was now. He must have been more determined, courageous, and free than he was now. He had once dreamed of time travel—before the war, a small number of people had travelled through wormholes, all young, strong men selected by the space agency. Some returned; some disappeared forever into the web-like currents of time. During the war, wormholes were widely used for attacks. After the war, wormhole travel was completely banned, and Number 536's fantasies of the past vanished. The instability of time and space had consequences; to this day, one or two people would still occasionally be swallowed by a suddenly appearing wormhole. Number 536 was twenty-three years old, newly a member of the Surveillance Division under the Deviance Control and Elimination Bureau. His job was simple: he only needed to identify behaviours threatening social stability on the nine monitors before him and report them immediately. Police from the Elimination Division would then correct these behaviours. Members of the Surveillance Division were forbidden from communicating with each other, so most of the time they stayed at home—like the last extinct chimpanzee years ago, spending its days in a cage. Each day, he woke in his residential module on the thirty-third floor; condensation on the ceiling left a tear on his face—like the residue of a grand, vast city's dream. He fed his attention to the cameras: a group of Class D workers dragging a train without power past the grain railway; a cold basement imprisoning a young Class A citizen; an elderly Class B woman repeatedly touching the refrigerator door, slipping her fingers into the crack, watching the blood flow as if reading a poem. Number 536 remembered the warmth of his mother's fingertips, but could not confirm whether she had truly existed, or if it was just an illusion of the incubator.
