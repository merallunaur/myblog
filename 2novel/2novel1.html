Francis stopped his work. A subtle change had invaded the still room. The white bud of the crab claw orchid trembled slightly, and a ladybug landed on it. The windows were shut tight—through which crack in this old house had it slipped in? "The kind of ladybug with seven black spots on its back is beneficial; it eats aphids. But the five-spotted kind that looks very similar is a pest; it will devour all the rice." This ladybug was of the seven-spotted variety. Francis placed a handkerchief between his thumb and forefinger, carefully pinched the insect, and lifted it. Through the thin silk, he could feel the tiny creature struggling, could even sense the minute movements of its six legs. He opened the window, released his fingers, and placed the ladybug on the outer sill. The ladybug fluttered its wings, bumped against the glass, and flew into the distance. The air was filled with the sweet fragrance of buttercream. Francis inhaled. The camphor leaves reaching into the window rustled softly. He rose from the creaking chair, left the room, descended the moss-carpeted stone steps, and walked into the pitch-black kitchen. Dusk had ended only minutes ago; the moon was still weaving through the clouds. The only light came through the open door, casting a faint blue shimmer over the dusty kitchenware. He poured himself a cup of hot water, leaned against the stove that had been cleaned that morning, and took stock of what still needed to be done. The evening air grew colder with autumn's arrival. The hot water was sweetened with honey—to protect his throat. He held the cup in his left hand and made small circles in the air with his right, as if an invisible list were unfurling before his eyes. Change the lightbulb—done. Air the quilts—done. Repair the stairs—done. Clean the house—almost. His grandfather had kept the room very tidy. A month ago, he had left this world without warning. A neighbour bringing blueberry pie discovered his body two hours after his breathing had stopped; he was still lying in his rocking chair, wearing his favourite burgundy cashmere sweater. That sweater had been on him for as long as Francis could remember. Even in the coffin, it remained—as if it had become a second skin. His grandfather had died with great dignity; not a drop of the black tea in his hand had spilled, and it was still warm. He had suffered a sudden stroke, yet his face bore an expression as if he had known all along. Serene and ruddy as a saint—the ideal appearance of the dead. That night, relatives gathered from all directions. Flustered, they poured out the tea from his hand, moved his body from the chair into the bedroom, then called the coffin shop to order the coffin his grandfather had commissioned upon entering old age. Then they calculated how much the funeral would cost. Among them was a financial advisor. 弗朗西斯停下手中的工作，某種細微變化入侵了靜止的房間。蟹爪蘭雪白的花蕾抖了一下，一隻瓢蟲落了上去。窗戶關緊了，是從這間老房子的哪條縫隙鑽進來的？「那種背上有七個黑點的瓢蟲是益蟲，牠會吃掉蚜蟲。而那種跟牠長得很像的五星瓢蟲是害蟲，牠會把稻子全部啃光。」這隻瓢蟲正是七星的。弗朗西斯將手帕墊在食指和拇指之間，小心翼翼地捏起牠。他的指腹能感覺到這小東西在那層薄薄的絲綢下掙扎，甚至能感覺到六隻腳細小的動作。他打開窗，鬆開手指，把瓢蟲放在窗框外沿。瓢蟲扇起翅膀，在玻璃窗上撞了一下，向遠處飛去。空氣中瀰漫著奶油的甜香，弗朗西斯吸了口氣，伸進窗子裡的香樟葉子發出一陣簌簌的晃蕩聲。他從嘎吱作響的椅子上起身，走出房門，走下鋪著苔蘚綠地毯的石台階，走進漆黑的廚房。黃昏結束不過幾分鐘，月亮尚在雲層間穿行。僅有的一點光線從敞開的大門透進來，廚具上蒙著閃爍淡淡藍光的灰塵。他給自己倒了一杯熱水，靠著早上剛剛擦乾淨的爐灶沿，盤算著還有哪些事情沒做。入秋後傍晚的空氣愈發冰冷，熱水中加了蜂蜜，這是為了保護他的嗓子。他把杯子握在左手中，右手在空中輕輕畫圈，彷彿那張看不見的清單正在他的眼前展開。更換燈泡，完成。晾曬被子，完成。修補樓梯，完成。打掃衛生，還差一點點。祖父把房間收拾得很乾淨，一個月前他毫無徵兆地離開人世。屍體在停止呼吸兩小時後被來送藍莓派的鄰居發現，還保持著躺在搖椅上的樣子，穿著他最常穿的那件酒紅色羊絨毛衣。這件毛衣從弗朗西斯有記憶那天起，祖父就一直穿著，直到躺進棺材，它在他身上停留的時間太久，彷彿他的第二層皮膚。祖父走得很端正，手裡捧著的紅茶沒有灑出一滴，尚且溫熱。他是因為中風突然去世的，臉上的表情卻像早就知道這件事。宛如聖父般的，紅潤而安詳，一個死者最為理想的樣貌。當天晚上，親屬們從各個地方趕來。他們手忙腳亂地倒掉他手裡的茶，把他的身體從椅子上搬進臥室裡，接著打電話給棺材店，叫他們送來祖父早在步入老年時就定好的那口棺材。緊接著，他們盤算起這場葬禮會花掉多少錢，他們之中有人是財務顧問。
The funeral proceeded without a hitch, occupying only three days of the relatives' time. Complex emotions chased and spread in the light of the vigil candles. He tried to calculate the proportions of their emotions from their varied expressions—grief or complaint. People reduced to a chart for analysis. Grandfather had left behind no significant possessions. No possessions, but he should have been proud. From this unremarkable country house, his sons and his sons' sons had rushed forth like gusts of fragrant wind, leaving this place behind. All had become societal elites. After earning his master's degree, Francis became a lawyer. Last month, he had won a labour dispute for a famous factory owner and was now on holiday. The task of sorting through the deceased's belongings had naturally fallen to him. Unlike any of his previous work, he deliberately proceeded slowly. His grandfather's diary—the flowering seasons of wild plants he had recorded, the hares and tits he had photographed with his old Leica. His delicate, hidden inner world now lay completely exposed before Francis's eyes. The traces of the deceased are difficult to erase, yet the living are often oblivious to this. When his grandfather was still alive, Francis had only visited him during summer and winter breaks. His grandmother had died before he was born. He had never imagined that his grandfather would one day leave. Like a brick in the house, his grandfather seemed never to have been young, yet never to have aged. He had never arranged anyone's life, yet all their lives had unfolded within his expectations. He had seen through them before they were even born; their souls were colourless to him. Transparent, fragile, like ice burning in the sun. 葬禮有條不紊地舉行，只花了親屬們三天。複雜的情緒在守靈蠟燭燃起的亮光中追逐蔓延。他試圖從形態各異的表情中算出他們情緒的比例。難過或是抱怨，人變成一張圖表供人分析。祖父沒有留下什麼財產。祖父沒有財產，但祖父應該驕傲。從這幢不起眼的鄉村小屋裡，他的兒子、兒子的兒子，他們像一陣又一陣夾雜著濃厚香味的風，倉促地掠過這個地方。他們都成為了社會精英。弗朗西斯在取得碩士學位後成為一名律師，上個月他幫一位有名的工廠主打贏了一場勞務糾紛，現在正在休假，遺物整理的任務自然而然地落在了他的頭上。不同於他以往的任何工作，他故意將這項工作推進得很慢。祖父的日記本，他所記錄的，野花的花期，他用老徠卡相機拍攝的野兔和山雀。他細膩而隱秘的內心如今毫無保留地暴露在他的視線下。逝者的痕跡很難去除，而生者往往對此渾然不覺。祖父還在世時，弗朗西斯只在寒暑假來看他，祖母在他出生前就已去世。他從未想過祖父有天會離開。就像房子的一塊磚，祖父彷彿從未年輕過，也彷彿從未變老。他從未安排過他們之間任何人的人生，他們所有人的人生又都在他的預料之內。他在他們出生之前就看透他們了，他們的靈魂對他來說是無色的。透明、脆弱，彷彿太陽下燃燒著的冰塊。
The sweetness of buttercream drifted into the house again. The neighbour's kitchen faced his window. The first person to discover his grandfather's body was the child of that household. His name was Arthur, seventeen years old, attending the local high school. They had met a few times before, when Francis was still in high school and Arthur was a little golden-haired ball of fluff who carried a rabbit doll and liked to sit on the steps counting stars and fireflies. At the funeral two days ago, Arthur had been crying. Francis had had no choice but to place his hand on his back. His thin shoulder blades trembled beneath his palm, as if he were the one who had lost a loved one. This evening, in the warm glow of the light, Arthur was dusting matcha powder onto a freshly baked cake. A dab of cream clung to his thumb; he put his thumb to his lips and licked it clean, like a cat grooming itself. 奶油的甜味又飄進房子，鄰居家的廚房正對著他的窗戶。第一個發現祖父屍體的是這家的孩子。他叫亞瑟，十七歲，在本地上高中。他們以前見過幾次面，那時弗朗西斯也還在上高中，而亞瑟是個抱著兔子玩偶，喜歡坐在台階上數星星和螢火蟲的金色小毛球。前兩天的葬禮中，亞瑟正在流淚，弗朗西斯別無選擇地把手放在他的背上。他瘦削的肩胛在他的手掌下顫抖，彷彿他才是失去親人的那個人。在這個傍晚，暖色的燈光中，他正往新鮮出爐的蛋糕上撒抹茶粉。一點奶油沾在他的拇指上，他把拇指放在嘴唇上，用舌頭舔著，彷彿梳理著毛髮的貓。
Francis sniffed, struck by an inexpressible, complex emotion. He sat on the floor, leaning against the stove. The moon had fallen asleep; the dark kitchen revealed nothing. His grandfather had been short in stature. The cup he had been holding in both hands at the moment of his death was now held in one hand by Francis. He pressed the teacup, decorated with a narcissus pattern, against his cheek, feeling the coldness and texture of the ceramic against his skin. Narcissus, crab claw orchid, seven-spot ladybug, camphor tree—the names of plants, the names of animals, the names of insects. A human body would eventually become food for insects. There was no one left to teach him the knowledge of the seasons. 弗朗西斯吸了吸鼻子，被難以用語言表達的複雜情緒擊中。他靠著灶台坐在地上。月亮睡著了，黑暗的廚房裡什麼都看不見。祖父身材矮小，他去世時雙手捧著的杯子，如今正被他一隻手握著。他把鑲有水仙花圖案的茶杯貼上自己的臉頰，用皮膚感受著陶瓷的冰冷與起伏。水仙花、蟹爪蘭、七星瓢蟲、香樟樹，植物的名字，動物的名字，昆蟲的名字，人的身體最終會成為昆蟲的食物，再也沒有人教給他四季的知識了。

Arthur felt a drop of water on his face. It slid along the lines of his skin across his cheek, gathered at his jaw, and dripped from his chin. He reached out, searching with his fingertips for the non-existent drop. After some fumbling, he confirmed it was an illusion. Most of his drowsiness had vanished, but he lay back down anyway, wrapping himself tighter in his blanket. The memory of discovering the body tormented him ceaselessly. In those few seconds of stunned stillness, his eyes had rapidly taken in every detail of that room. But in subsequent recollections, only dreamlike, massive blocks of colour remained—like a child's crayon drawing. Burgundy, moss green, bright yellow. Blurry, vivid, and cold. He had known old Mr. Bonnefoy, had even been his friend. One Christmas, he had given Arthur a handmade garden gnome doll. That doll still stood in his garden, at the very front of the other gnomes. It was the leader of the gnome army, so proud. Even though old Mr. Bonnefoy had never said so, Arthur knew that this gnome leader should have belonged to someone else. The old gentleman loved his grandson dearly, yet rarely showed it. Over the years of growing up, Arthur had heard the legends of his grandson countless times—how he had left the town, how he had advanced through school, how he had graduated, how he had achieved success. He was a symbol, a teaching tool, a ruler by which to plan one's life. But whenever Arthur thought of him, spoke his name—Francis—he remained as he had been at the very beginning of Arthur's memory. That memory was as clear as the veins of a leaf. He had once sat on the steps and watched Francis ride his grandfather's old bicycle, hurtling down the steep slope, his shining golden hair flying in the wind, a wildflower of unknown name tucked behind his ear. At old Mr. Bonnefoy's passing, Arthur felt regret and helplessness, as if witnessing a garden wither. What he feared was not the stiffness and change of the human body, nor the so-called passing of life. He had never truly known this life; perhaps no one had. Yet its traces evaporated into the dry, cold autumn air, erased like a child's crayon drawing. 亞瑟感覺到有水滴在他的臉上，那滴水順著皮膚的紋路流過臉頰，從下頜匯集到下巴。他伸出手，用指尖尋找著那不存在的水滴。一番摸索後，他確認了這是自己的錯覺。睡意消失了大半，但他仍舊躺了回去，用毯子裹緊自己的身體。第一次發現屍體的記憶一刻不停地刺激著他。在他愣神的幾秒間，眼睛曾飛快地接收了那間屋子的所有細節。但往後的回憶中只剩夢一般的巨大色塊，彷彿孩子的塗鴉。酒紅、苔綠、明黃。模糊、豔麗而冰冷。他認識老波諾弗瓦先生，甚至可以說是他的朋友。某一年的聖誕節，他送了他一個手工地精娃娃。那個娃娃還擺在他的花園裡，在其他地精娃娃的最前面。它是一支地精隊伍的領導者，它那麼神氣。即使老波諾弗瓦先生沒有明說，他也知道這個地精領袖本該屬於一個其他什麼人。那位老先生很愛自己的孫子，卻對此鮮有表現。在成長的時光裡，亞瑟無數次聽過他孫子的傳說。他是如何走出小鎮、如何幾次升學、如何畢業，又是如何獲得成功。他是一個符號、一件教具、一種用來規劃人生的尺子。但每當亞瑟想起他、念出弗朗西斯這個名字，他仍舊是他記憶的起點中的樣子。他對此的記憶如葉脈般清晰。他曾經坐在台階上，看見過弗朗西斯騎著祖父的舊自行車，從陡峭的山坡頂呼嘯而下，閃亮的金髮在空中飛揚，耳邊別著一枝叫不出名字的野花。對於老波諾弗瓦先生的離去，他感到懊悔和無助，彷彿目睹一座花園的枯萎。他所恐懼的並非人體的僵硬與變化，也不是所謂生命的消逝。他還沒有認真地了解過這個生命，也許沒有人認真地了解過這個生命，它的痕跡卻蒸發在秋天乾冷的空氣中，像孩子的塗鴉一般被擦去了。
Now, dawn silently invaded the bedroom. He felt his two feet, covered by the blanket, gradually grow warm. The body heat stolen by emotion slowly returned to him. Amid a hazy chorus of birdsong, he fell asleep again. 現在，黎明無聲地侵入這間臥室。他感覺自己被毯子蓋著的兩隻腳逐漸變熱，被情緒奪走的體溫逐漸回到他的身體。在一陣朦朧的鳥鳴聲中，他再度進入夢鄉。

Someone had once died on the spot where he was standing. Francis looked up at the sky and lifted a maple leaf he had picked up from the ground. Through the hole gnawed by an insect, a flock of geese entered his field of vision. He was leaning against the wall, smoking, when his thoughts were suddenly seized by a past memory. Someone had once died where he stood. He must have been about five or six then. His favourite food was Trdelník—a long baked bread sprinkled with cinnamon, walnut, and almond powder, its inner walls thick with chocolate spread. If he ate too much, he would be woken at night by toothaches. Only a Czech immigrant who lived in the deepest house on Market Street made this bread. Francis could no longer remember his name, only that its pronunciation was strange and difficult, the letters connected by some kind of animal's roar. When he lifted the dough and slammed it onto the flour-dusted counter, it was like pushing a stone up a mountain. One Trdelník cost one and a half euros. He would walk through the smells of fish, rotting vegetables, and meat to buy it—his memories of that place always ended with odours. That Czech man had frozen to death because of alcoholism. The misfortune happened right at the foot of those old grey walls. The body lay face-down; there were a few bruises on the forehead from the fall. It had snowed all night, hiding the scents of the living. The next morning, an elderly man clearing snow found him. By then it was too late. The police came and cordoned off the area with yellow tape. A day or two later, the tape was removed. When spring came and the snow melted, the grey wall had been washed clean by the snowmelt, neat as new. Francis had been five years old. He did not know what death meant. He only knew that he would never eat Trdelník again. 他所站著的那塊地方曾經有人死去過。弗朗西斯抬頭望向天空，舉起一片在地上撿起的楓葉。透過被蟲子咬出的縫隙，一群大雁進入他的視野。他正靠在牆上吸菸，思維毫無徵兆地被昔日回憶攫取。他所站的地方曾有人死去。那時他大概五六歲，他最愛吃的是塔塔蜜——某種撒著肉桂粉、核桃粉和杏仁粉的長烤麵包，內壁抹著厚厚的巧克力醬。如果吃得太多，夜裡就會被牙疼驚醒。只有住在菜市街最裡一幢房子的一位捷克移民會做這種麵包，弗朗西斯已記不清他的名字，只記得它的發音奇怪又拗口，字母靠著某種動物的吼聲連結。當他舉起麵糰，摔在撒滿麵粉的案板上，彷彿正推著一塊石頭上山。一個塔塔蜜一點五歐元，他舉著麵包穿過魚腥味、腐爛的菜葉味、肉類的臭氣，對這裡的記憶以氣味結束。那個捷克人，他因為酗酒被凍死，厄運就發生在這片古老的灰色牆根底下。屍體臉朝下地倒著，額頭上有幾處摔倒時的瘀傷。下了一整夜的雪，生者的氣味被積雪掩藏。第二天早上，掃雪的老人發現了他。那時為時已晚，警察趕來，在周圍拉起警戒線。過了一兩天，警戒線被拆走。春天積雪融化，灰色的牆根被雪水沖洗過，整潔如新。弗朗西斯五歲大，他不知道死亡意味著什麼，他只知道以後吃不到了。
A sharp sting brought Francis back to the present. Ash had fallen on the back of his hand. He sighed and shook it off. The excessive sweetness of that memory was now spreading across his taste buds—it would have been far better than the rust-and-jasmine-flavoured instant coffee from the office machine, but the smoke had dulled his palate. He took the cigarette between his lips, inhaled deeply, pushed the taste down into his lungs with his nose, and let it out through his mouth. Whenever he did this, his mind grew clearer. 刺痛使弗朗西斯回過神來，菸灰掉在他的手背上。他嘆了口氣，甩甩手將菸灰甩掉。記憶中那陣過分的甜味此時正在他的味蕾上擴散，應該比公司咖啡機帶有鐵鏽與茉莉香精味的即溶咖啡好得多，只是菸味使他的味覺變得遲鈍了。他把菸含進嘴裡，深深吸了一口，用鼻子把那陣味道壓進肺裡去，再從嘴裡吐出來。每當他做這件事，頭腦就愈發清醒。
His first case: a rich kid had run over a homeless man. Due to lack of evidence, the killer could not be convicted. His family had paid to have the surveillance footage deleted before the case even went to trial. Francis had played almost no role in this case, yet he still received a large sum of money. The homeless man had no family. Francis had visited the columbarium to see his urn. Tens of thousands of identical, fist-sized urns stood in neat rows on towering shelves, stretching from the entrance into the abyss of darkness in the distance. Unclaimed souls. He had tried to walk in, to find his ashes, or to be interred somewhere, but nearly lost his way. At the deepest part of the columbarium stood a weeping statue of the Virgin Mary, the brightest spot in the entire hall. He had lingered there, gazing down at the countless dead, and afterwards eaten cream and bacon pasta. In the end, he never found the homeless man's ashes. He walked closer, squinted one eye from a few metres away, took aim, and tossed the butt in. He was about to walk home when he heard someone shout something at him. He patted his shoulder, offering a reassuring smile. "I was too absorbed and didn't see you. I'm sorry." 「我太入神了，沒有注意到你，對不起。」
"No, I'm the one who should apologise." 「我才是，抱歉。」
Arthur waved his hands in a fluster; when anxious, he could barely speak. He dared not look into his eyes. Francis's eyes—they were like tunnels leading to weariness, desire, and unease. If he looked into them too long, his self would suddenly perish in that silence so chilling it made one's blood run cold. Arthur forced a smile, wrapped his arms around himself, and sought out some deep tremor with his fingertips. 亞瑟慌慌張張地擺手，一著急就更說不出話來。他不敢看他的眼睛。他的眼睛，弗朗西斯的眼睛，彷彿是通向倦意、慾望與不安的隧道。如果看著他的眼睛太久，自我就會在那雙沉默得令人膽寒的眼睛中遽然而逝。亞瑟勉強笑著，用雙臂抱住自己，從指尖探尋到了某種深處的悸動。
"Shall I give you a ride home?" 「要我載你回家嗎？」
Francis's voice roused him. Without waiting for an answer, he reached out and adjusted the seat height—he liked it set high, like a mountain bike. He inevitably thought of the past, of the times he had bruised and bloodied himself from this very habit. He was grateful it was only a bicycle that had hit him; he couldn't afford to be injured now. Arthur did not refuse his offer, especially since his knee was swelling uncomfortably. He sat sideways on the back seat, gripping the front edge. Francis mounted the bike, flicked the bell with his thumb, and gripped the handlebars firmly, as if seeking to earn the other's trust. 弗朗西斯的聲音喚醒了他。他沒有等他回答就伸出手調整自行車的坐墊，他喜歡把坐墊調得像山地車那樣高，他不可避免地想起從前，因為這個愛好摔得鼻青臉腫的時候。他慶幸撞到他的是輛自行車，如今他承受不了受傷的代價。亞瑟沒有拒絕他的提議，何況膝蓋正毫不客氣地腫脹著。他側坐在後座上，抓著前座邊緣。弗朗西斯跨上自行車，用拇指撥了撥鈴鐺。他穩穩當當地握住車把，彷彿想得到對方的信任。
"You'd better hold on tight. We're at the top of the hill now." 「你要抓穩了，我們現在可是在山頂。」

"I'm ready." 「我坐穩了。」

A flower snatched away by a titmouse, a fruit falling and rotting, a leaf being painfully gnawed by a caterpillar—everything was carried on the slope's back, walking in silent haste. His eyes shattered into a thousand shards of glass on the ground. The slope stretched endlessly. When he had first learned to ride a bike, this slope had seemed the longest in the world; riding from the top to the bottom had taken his entire childhood. Once, on a holiday, he had gone to the Alps. He had taken the cable car down; it had taken only a few minutes. "I suppose we will." 「我想是會的。」

He imagined some kind of change, some kind of return, as he imagined a bird returning to its nest. Green feathers with the sheen of silk. Tonight, he did not want the light. 弗朗西斯拍拍他的肩膀，不小心碰到他鎖骨上方露出的皮膚。他的鎖骨中心有一顆小痣。他正拿雨水般的目光看著他，他的呼吸和一顆會呼吸的痣。弗朗西斯移開目光，對他溫柔地道了晚安。他目送著他走進家門，在二層的臥室亮起黯淡的黃色燈光。然後他回到漆黑一片的房子，躺在祖父去世的那張搖椅上。他想像著某種變化，某種回歸，就像他想像一隻鳥兒回到自己的巢窠。有綢緞光澤的綠色羽毛。今晚他不想要燈光。

What he now held in his hand was the handkerchief Francis had tied around his knee. There was a scent of some perfume, stitched between the silk threads. He soaked it in cold water, and the fragrance spread like a gasoline film on rainwater. Unlike any scented flower he knew, this fragrance carried a heavy, powdery note, a long-lasting Oriental tone. When he inhaled that nameless scent left beside him, it was as if a hand with red fingernails was stroking his nose. He pinched the handkerchief again from the cold water and rubbed the bloodstained part with his knuckle. The salt in the water stung and itched, but the stain soon disappeared. A few minutes later, Arthur raised the handkerchief to check for any remaining mark. Daylight passed through the fabric and into his eyes, giving his irises a milky, silken texture. Dogs barked in the street—from the sound, about five or six months old. It was already noon. Fewer and fewer people walked their dogs in the morning; the cold dawn air kept them indoors. Arthur placed a sheet of scratch paper on the windowsill and laid the handkerchief over it to dry. Studying was so boring; his mind kept wandering. The season for raspberries and blackberries was July; by September they lost their flavour due to dehydration. But he still ate a lot. When he ate sweets, he forgot everything. One piece remained. He asked his mother if he could give it to the neighbour. With her permission, he fetched his favourite plate—a green dish decorated with a bird—and placed the tart on it. He returned to the second floor to retrieve the handkerchief, which had dried as stiff as the berries. He dug an unused iron out of the cupboard, moved it over the handkerchief, and nearly burned himself. How should he fold it? The hand holding the plate seemed never to have held anything heavier. Francis held his grandfather's notebook in his hands. He had memorised its contents within the first few days of his arrival. His memory was one of the things he prided himself on; otherwise, he could not have become a lawyer. Extracting simple, straightforward justice from the dense, obscure sea of words was not something easily accomplished by those lacking memory or perseverance. Yet he could not determine when or in what form justice became twisted. When he held that thick, old notebook, it was as if he were holding a Bible. When Arthur knocked, Francis was looking at the wildflowers his grandfather had painted by hand. His paintings were as clumsy and innocent as a kindergartener's, relaxing his mood unconsciously. At the soft knocking, he closed the notebook and walked to the door. He didn't need to check through the peephole to know who it was; he recognised Arthur's footsteps. The final sound of each step had a faint friction that others' lacked, as if two sleepy insects were stuck to his heels. Now with his injured knee, that friction grew longer. He opened the door, thanked Arthur, took the plate from his hands, and put it in the fridge. He hadn't eaten lunch, but he didn't feel hungry. 亞瑟敲門時他正在看著祖父生前手繪的野花。他的畫像幼兒園孩子般拙劣而天真，使他的心情不自覺地放鬆。在輕飄飄的敲門聲中，他闔上本子，向門口走去。他無需透過貓眼就能確定來者的身份，他認得亞瑟的腳步聲。每一聲的最後會比其他人多一種細微的摩擦。彷彿有兩隻瞌睡蟲，牢牢地黏在他的腳後跟上。如今他的膝蓋負傷了，某一聲的摩擦變得更長。他打開門，道謝後接過亞瑟手裡的盤子，把它放進冰箱。他沒吃午飯，但他感覺不到餓。
"Your handkerchief." 「你的手帕。」
Arthur held out the handkerchief with both hands. Francis was staring into his eyes. Yet Arthur felt he wasn't really looking at his eyes. His irises were violet handles of a knife; the gaze from his pupils was a blade swinging down at him. He felt a sting deep inside. Those eyes were gouging his entrails. Arthur stood too close to him. The discordant, exotic scent on him was now mixed with a damp, woody quality. His tall, cool body revealed a soft, nostalgic quality that captivated Arthur. 亞瑟用雙手把手帕遞到弗朗西斯的手中，對方正盯著自己的眼睛看。他卻感覺他不是在看他的眼睛。他的虹膜是紫羅蘭色的刀柄，瞳孔中射出的目光是向他一揮而下的刀刃。他的深處感受到一股刺痛，那雙眼睛在剜他的內臟。亞瑟和他離得太近了，他身上那股不和諧的異域香氣此時夾雜著潮濕的木質氣息，高大冷淡的軀體顯現出一種柔軟的懷舊特質，令他心馳神往。
"Thank you." 「謝謝。」
Francis took the handkerchief and tucked it into his breast pocket. Arthur stood before him, his head raised. Light and shadow danced like a small bird, moving with the tilt of his head. In a trance, he reached out and touched Arthur's jet-black eyelashes. It was the season when clematis vitalba bloomed; that colour suited his eyes well. 弗朗西斯接過手帕，塞進自己胸前的口袋。亞瑟抬著頭站在他面前，光影像一隻小鳥，隨著他偏頭的動作跳動。他在一片恍惚之中伸出手，觸碰他濃黑色的睫毛。葡萄葉鐵線蓮怒放的季節，那顏色很襯他的眼睛。
"Take a walk with me." 「陪我走走吧。」
He made the invitation and waited for an answer. Arthur agreed hastily, placing the textbook he had used to hold the handkerchief on the shoe cabinet by the entrance. They stepped out into the sun-drenched afternoon air. Francis put his arm around Arthur's shoulder, leaning some of his weight on him. 他邀約道，等待一個回答。亞瑟慌慌張張地答應下來，把方才用來夾手帕的教科書放在進門的鞋櫃上。他們相繼走入午後陽光充沛的空氣中，弗朗西斯攬過亞瑟的肩，以便他把一部分體重倚靠在自己身上。
"But... where are we going?" 「但是…我們要去哪裡呢？」
"You came along without knowing?" 「你不知道就跟來了嗎？」
Francis turned his head and smiled. He wanted him nearby, even if it was just to follow him resentfully. But he didn't want to make him angry. So he stopped smiling and tightened his hand on Arthur's shoulder slightly. 弗朗西斯轉過頭輕笑。他想讓他跟在周圍，即使是滿懷慍怒地跟著。但他並不想惹他生氣。於是他收起笑臉，微微收緊放在亞瑟肩膀上的手。
"In the future, you'd better not go out with strange strangers." 「以後最好少跟來路不明的陌生人出去。」
Arthur looked at him. Those green eyes never left him, searching for his presence. They were so childishly exposed. Then, the presence his eyes took in coalesced into words, falling as softly as a feather. 亞瑟看向他，那雙綠色的眼睛一直落在他身上，探尋著他的氣息。眼睛就這麼稚氣地暴露著。接著，他眼睛所接收到的氣息就匯成語言，像一片羽毛般輕柔落下。
"But you're not a stranger. You're Mr. Bonnefoy's grandson. You're Francis." 「但是，你不是陌生人。你是波諾弗瓦先生的孫子，你是弗朗西斯。」
As soon as he finished speaking, he put his hand to his face. Coolness spread over his eyelids, his cheeks. He really did look like someone with a fever. 他剛說完就把手擱在自己的臉上，涼意漫上他的眼皮，他的臉頰。他真像一個發燒的人。
"May I ride your bicycle?" 「我可以騎你的自行車嗎？」
"Yes, but you can't break it. It was my fourteenth birthday present." 「可以，但是你不能把它弄壞，那是我十四歲的生日禮物。」
So they dragged the bicycle out from under the shed. The seat hadn't been adjusted since that day. Francis mounted it, and Arthur sat behind him, gripping the back edge of the seat with both hands. 於是他們從車棚底下拖出那輛自行車，座墊從那天之後就沒再調過。弗朗西斯跨上去，而亞瑟坐在他身後，兩隻手握著前座後緣。
"If you're scared, hold onto my waist." 「如果你感到害怕，就抓住我的腰。」
Arthur's hands stayed on the seat. The bicycle passed through the drowsy town. A few houses still smelled of lunch; more were silent. The air was like a lazy folk song, and he felt it entering his brain through all his senses, making him drowsy too. The road out of town wound tortuously. They passed the hill from a few days earlier. Where Francis couldn't pedal, he got off and pushed. His sleeves were pushed above his elbows, his forearm muscles tensing. With those arms, he began to create a world. A transparent river flowed between them and the town, meandering slowly. The source of that river was all the years Francis had spent as a stranger in a strange land. 亞瑟的手仍舊抓著前座，自行車穿過昏昏欲睡的小鎮，有幾座屋子仍舊散發著午飯的氣味，更多的沉默著。空氣像一首懶洋洋的民謠，他覺得這首民謠正通過所有感覺器官進入他的腦子，他也變得昏昏欲睡起來。通向鎮外的路蜿蜒曲折，他們經過了前幾天經過的那座山坡，在騎不動的地方弗朗西斯就下車推著他走。他的袖子被推到手臂上方，小臂肌肉繃緊。用那雙手臂，他開始創造一個世界。有一條透明的河隔在他們和小鎮中間，緩緩流淌。那條河的源頭是弗朗西斯作為異鄉人的所有時光。
"We're here." 「我們到了。」
Francis stood before the signpost marking the edge of town. That sign might have been there since his grandfather's childhood. Vines of some unknown plant were wound around it—his grandfather had never taught him their name. He pulled the vines down with both hands, revealing the peeling red paint beneath, like lifting the shy veil of a bride no longer young. He reached out, feeling the rusted letters with his palm. "Welcome back home." 弗朗西斯站在出鎮的指示牌前。那塊指示牌說不定在他祖父小時候就已經立在那裡，上面不知纏繞著什麼植物的藤蔓，祖父還沒教過他。他雙手扯下那些藤條，露出紅漆剝落的本體。彷彿掀開一位不再年輕的新娘的羞怯面紗。他伸出手去，用手掌感受著長著鐵鏽的一個個字母。「歡迎再次回家。」
"Francis, but there's nothing here." 「弗朗西斯，可是這裡什麼都沒有。」
As if he hadn't heard, Francis was again immersed in memories of the past. It wasn't until Arthur tugged at his clothes that he came back to himself. He crouched down, picked the clematis vitalba growing beside the sign, and held it in his hand. 沒有聽到似的，弗朗西斯又一次沉溺於對過去的回憶。直到亞瑟拉拉他的衣服，他才回過神來，旋即蹲下身子，採下路牌旁的葡萄葉鐵線蓮，攥在手裡。
"There's nothing." 「什麼都沒有。」
He repeated Arthur's words like a sleepwalker. He asked if he could leave the bicycle here, and received an affirmative answer. He put his arm around Arthur's shoulder again, stepped over the ankle-high wild grass, and headed up the mountain. The town's exit led to an even higher hill. 他囈語般地重複了亞瑟的話。詢問他是否能把自行車放在這裡，得到了肯定的答覆。他再次攬過他的肩，從超過腳踝的野草上跨過去，走向上山的路。鎮的出口是一座更高的山。
"Does your knee still hurt?" 「你的膝蓋還疼嗎？」
"No." 「不疼了。」
He lied. Francis broke off two willow branches and wove them into rings, then threaded the clematis through. 他撒了謊。弗朗西斯折下兩條柳枝，將它們編成圓環。再把鐵線蓮插上去。
"Have you ever been to the top of the mountain?" 「你上過山頂嗎？」
"Of course." 「當然了。」
"At night?" 「在晚上呢？」
"I've been then too." 「也來過。」
"I've never been." 「我從沒來過。」
Francis looked at him. The crystalline transparency of their eyes was so similar. Arthur's gaze was searching, falling like a soft, warm velvet cushion. Francis's gaze, however, was appraising. Anyone would be pierced by that translucent, deep purple dagger. It passed through the body without leaving a trace, even before one felt its presence. It was as if he were observing the funeral of a stranger—a detached onlooker. His was the gaze of an outsider. 弗朗西斯看著他，他們眼睛水晶般的通透質地如此類似，亞瑟的目光帶著探尋，彷彿落在某塊絲絨墊子上，柔軟而溫熱。而弗朗西斯的目光具有審視的特質，任何人都會被那把晶瑩剔透的深紫色匕首刺穿。甚至在感覺到它的存在前，它就那麼不著痕跡地穿過身體。他彷彿在端詳陌生人的葬禮。一個冷漠的旁觀者。他的目光是局外人的目光。
"Vetch." 「野豌豆。」
"Crane's-bill." 「老鸛草。」
"Pansy." 「三色堇。」
In an instant, he withdrew that gaze. The illusion of that appraisal burst like a bubble. Francis bent down again, picked wildflowers from the ground, and wove them into the willow rings. With each flower he picked, he announced its name, sometimes in Latin. Arthur followed behind him. The sun set again. In the pitch darkness, he felt Francis take his hand. The warmth of that hand passed through his skin without end, as if the hand were independent, belonging to no body. Their conversation grew sparse as they walked toward the summit in the silence. 剎那間他收回了那道目光，方才那場審視的錯覺如泡沫般破裂。弗朗西斯又彎下腰，採起地上的野花，插進編好的柳條環裡。每採一朵就報出它們的名字，有時又說著拉丁語。亞瑟跟在他身後，太陽又落下去了。漆黑一片中，他感覺到弗朗西斯拉住他的手。那隻手的溫度透過他的皮膚源源不斷地傳過來，彷彿這隻手是獨立的，不屬於一個人的身體。他們的交談變少了，在少言寡語中走向山頂。
"Have you ever thought about the future?" 「你曾想過未來嗎？」
"Of course. I want to go farther than the city centre." 「當然想過。我要去比城中心更遠的地方。」
Francis looked in the direction Arthur pointed. On the highway, a truck slowly drove out of the mountain pass, like a ladybug crawling on a weed. All things fell into a state of turbulent stillness. He could hear the breathing of plants straining to grow, desperate for sunlight. Seedlings tangled in vines died. A fungus pierced an ant's body, sprouting from its head. He wished he had a bottle of wine, but all he had was the woven wildflower crown. 弗朗西斯順著亞瑟手指的那塊地方看去，公路上一輛貨車緩緩駛出山口，彷彿一隻瓢蟲在雜草上爬行。萬物陷入一種洶湧的靜止狀態，他能感覺到植物為了爭取陽光而拼命長大的呼吸聲。樹苗被藤蔓纏繞而死去。某種真菌刺進螞蟻的身體，從頭部伸出來。他希望自己能有一瓶酒，但手邊只有他編好的野花花環。
"Like London?" 「比如倫敦？」
"I don't know." 「我不知道。」
Francis took the cigarette pack from his pocket but only ran his finger over the cigarettes. He understood that what Arthur pursued was a kind of hazy distance, an image. Like the yearning for the East during the Age of Discovery—the East drawn on paper from vague information, the East of gold and temptation, its details added daily in pencil. "The East." 弗朗西斯把菸盒從口袋裡掏出來，只是用手指在菸上劃過。他明白了，亞瑟所追求的是一種朦朧的遠方，一個意象。就像大航海時代對東方的嚮往。靠著一點模糊的資料在紙上描繪的東方，那個充滿黃金與誘惑的東方。每天都用鉛筆添著細節，「東——方——」。
"I live in a place like that." 「我住在那種地方。」
"Is it good?" 「好嗎？」
"Can't say good or bad." 「談不上好壞。」
He had no intention of shattering the other's illusion, nor did he wish to preach condescendingly from the vantage point of age. 他沒有打破對方幻想的意思，也不願作為年長者居高臨下地規勸。
"Living with people is difficult. I feel I have a kind of passive sense of justice." 「和人一起生活很困難。我覺得我有一種消極的正義感。」
"But that's good, Francis. You're a good person." 「可是那很好，弗朗西斯，你是個好人。」
"Is that so?" 「是嗎？」
He smiled, then continued in a voice too low for anyone to hear. 他微笑了一下，接著用誰都聽不到的聲音說下去。
"If you go too far too fast, you'll fall on the slope." 「如果走得太遠又太快，就會在坡上跌倒。」
"Francis, a shooting star!" 「弗朗西斯，是流星！」
Arthur suddenly cried out. Francis turned to look at him. He had already closed his eyes, his hands pressed together in a praying posture. In that flash of light, Francis suddenly wanted to lean in and look at his face—his dark eyelashes quivering slightly, believing so fervently in that celestial phenomenon. 亞瑟突然大叫道，弗朗西斯轉頭看向他，他已經閉上了眼睛，雙手交叉在一起，擺出一個祈禱的姿勢。一瞬光亮中，他突然想湊近看看他的臉，他黑漆漆的睫毛微微顫晃著，對那種天文現象深信不疑。
"Did you make your wish?" 「許好願了嗎？」
"Mm." 「嗯。」
Arthur crawled forward on his knees, as if the accident a few days ago had happened to someone else. He drew close to Francis, pressing against him as he sat down. He hummed a soft, gentle nursery rhyme. Francis fumbled in the darkness, raised the flower crown, and placed it on Arthur's head like a tiara. The scent of flowers brushed his nose. It made Arthur lean on him, forcing him to lie down. The former laughed, then rolled over like a cat, moved off him, and lay down beside him. 亞瑟用膝蓋跪著走了幾步，彷彿前幾天的車禍發生在其他人身上。他靠近他，緊貼著他坐下。輕聲哼起一首輕柔的童謠。弗朗西斯在黑暗中四處摸索，將花環舉起來，像一頂王冠一樣戴在亞瑟的頭上。他的鼻尖飄過花的香氣，使亞瑟壓在他身上迫使他躺下了。而前者笑了笑，旋即像貓一樣打了個滾，從他身上起開，躺在他的身側。
"I feel like I might just fall asleep like this tonight." 「我覺得今天說不定會這麼睡著。」
Arthur turned his head, looked into his eyes, and broke into a cheerful smile. Moonlight streamed through the gaps in the flower crown on his head, his face bathed in floating silver radiance, every expression moving with clumsy delight. Then he suddenly stopped smiling and looked into his eyes again. Those eyes searched within him, stirring a memory somewhere in Francis's heart. Forest-like, emerald-shining green. First they rejoiced in his eyes, then fell on his shoulders. In this moment, he understood the whisper contained in Arthur's gaze. In this moment, the flowers found their voice. 亞瑟轉過頭，看著他的眼睛，展露出一個開懷的笑臉。月光穿過他頭上花環的縫隙，他的臉沐浴在浮動的銀色光輝中，每一種神情都笨拙而快活地移動著。他突然又不笑了，再次看向他的眼睛。這雙眼睛在他身上探尋著，在他心中某處激起了回憶。森林般的、綠寶石般閃耀的綠色。先是在他的眼睛裡歡騰，又落在他的肩上。此刻，他聽懂了他眼神中飽含的私語。此刻，花有了語言。

He moistened a paper towel with water and pressed it to the scab on his knee. After a moment, he removed the towel and threw it in the bin. Then he pried at the edge of the scab with his fingernail and peeled it off. The last bit of attachment still hurt. The newly grown skin was pink as a baby's cheek. Soon, it would be just like everywhere else. In three months, he wouldn't even notice a scar there. He'd been feeling listless lately, falling behind in his schoolwork. Perhaps it was because the house across the street was about to be empty again. He often heard faint sounds in the night—the scraping of furniture. Those sounds fell on him like a drop of rain or an insect, keeping him from sleep. 他正用水潤濕紙巾，敷在自己膝蓋的痂上。一會兒，他拿下那塊紙巾，丟進垃圾桶裡。接著，他用指甲撬著那塊痂的邊緣，把它撕下來。最後的連接處還是很疼，新長出的皮膚是嬰兒臉頰般的粉色。過不了多久，就會變得跟其他地方一樣了。三個月後，他甚至都不會覺得那裡有塊疤。他最近總提不起神，功課落下了不少。也許是因為對街的房子又要空下來了。他常在半夜裡聽見微弱的，桌椅移動的聲音。那聲音像一滴雨或是一隻蟲子似的落在他身上。他因為那聲音夜不能寐。
After they came down from the mountain that day, he hadn't seen Francis again. The other had never contacted him. It seemed he had taken on a new case and been forced to end his holiday early. What was that case about? Arthur hadn't asked, afraid to know. He was afraid of the unknown person, afraid that the gentle dagger would ultimately be turned on himself. In his daze, he liked to stare at the window of the house across the street, as if the house were a living creature, Francis in its belly, old Mr. Bonnefoy in heaven, his gaze moving along the spine of this being. He watched the shifting figures in the house, felt a wave of panic, and stroked the flower crown hanging by his bed. He had kept it all this time. The flowers had all withered. 那天他們從山頂下來之後，他就沒再見過弗朗西斯。對方從沒聯繫過他。而且似乎也接到了新案子，被迫提前結束了休假。那個案子是關於什麼的呢，他沒有問，也害怕知道。他害怕他不認識的某個人，害怕那把溫柔的匕首會最終刺向他自己。他在發呆時喜歡看著對面房子的窗戶，彷彿這座房子是一種生物，弗朗西斯在牠的肚子裡，而老波諾弗瓦先生在天上，目光在這個生靈的脊柱上移動著。他看著房子裡晃動著的人影，感到一陣恐慌，撫摸起掛在床頭的花環。他一直留著它，花已經全部枯萎了。
His intuition told him that today was the day Francis was leaving. So he slipped out of his room and walked for the last time toward the house across the street, its door standing open. The house was cleaner than before, almost metallic in its sheen, like leaves washed by snowmelt. He went upstairs, pushed open the door. Francis was sitting in a chair, his forehead resting on his hand, a cigarette in the other. There was a tired fine line beneath his eyes. 他的直覺告訴他今天是弗朗西斯離開的日子。於是他溜出房間，最後一次走向對面敞開著門的房子。房子比以前更加乾淨了，彷彿被雪水洗過的樹葉般幾乎閃著金屬光澤。他走上樓，推開門，弗朗西斯坐在椅子上扶著額頭，一隻手拿著菸。他的眼睛下面有一道疲憊的細紋。
"What are you going to do with the house?" 「你打算怎麼處理這間房子？」
"Just leave it empty. You wouldn't want anyone else moving in, would you? It would be awful if some noisy children came." 「就這麼空著，你也不想讓別人住進去吧，萬一來幾個吵鬧的孩子就糟了。」
Arthur turned and saw Francis looking at him with a gaze that sought to reassure him. He had stubbed out his cigarette the moment Arthur walked in. The window was open; the camphor leaves were still green. How much further would they grow in time? 亞瑟轉頭便發現弗朗西斯正用凝望的眼神想要讓他安心，他在他走進來的那一刻就把菸按滅了。他開著窗戶，香樟樹的葉子仍是綠油油的，以後又會長到什麼程度呢？
"Grandfather is gone. Neither of us has any reason to come back anymore." 「祖父走了，我們誰也沒有再回來的理由了。」
He was the one who had discovered the body. Arthur suddenly felt the same way. Everything in the room began to twist again; even the moving, speaking figure before him became a living block of colour. "I don't think so." 「我想是不會了。」
Arthur wanted to say more, but felt that all his words were like glass wrapped in paper, dropped into a lake. It seemed he could only fall into that lake, to be with it for a long, long time. 亞瑟還想說什麼，卻覺得所有文字都像被包裹著的玻璃，掉入了一片湖水中。他好像只能墜入湖水，與湖水長久相伴。
"Be well, Mr. Bonnefoy." 「祝你健康，波諾弗瓦先生。」
"You too, Arthur." 「你也是，亞瑟。」

① Trdelník: The true origin of this pastry is Hungary's Kürtőskalács (meaning "chimney cake"), whose history dates back to the 18th century as a traditional food of the Székely people in Hungary. The traditional Hungarian version uses a simple dough (flour, yeast, sugar, salt, etc.), which is fermented, wrapped around a wooden spit, baked, and finally sprinkled only with granulated sugar. At the end of the 20th century and beginning of the 21st, this pastry was introduced to the Czech Republic, particularly Prague, where it underwent "modernisation" to cater to tourist tastes. In Czech, it is called Trdelník, a name derived from the wooden roller ("trdlo") used to make it. The simple sugar coating was upgraded to a mixture of cinnamon sugar, ground walnuts, and ground almonds, and the hollow interior was filled with ice cream, cream, chocolate sauce, strawberries, and other fillings—a spectacularly photogenic invention. Many Czechs will point out that this filled version of Trdelník is not a traditional Czech food, but rather a 21st-century "tourist invention." ① 塔塔蜜（Trdelnik），這種甜點的真正源頭是匈牙利的 Kürtőskalács（意為「煙囪蛋糕」），其歷史可追溯到18世紀，是匈牙利塞凱伊人的傳統美食。傳統的匈牙利做法麵團成分簡單（麵粉、酵母、糖、鹽等），發酵後纏繞在木棍上烤製，最後只撒上砂糖。在20世紀末至21世紀初，這種甜點被引入捷克，尤其是在布拉格，為了迎合遊客口味而進行了「現代化改造」。它在捷克被稱為 Trdelník，這個名字來源於製作它使用的木製滾筒（「trdlo」）。從簡單的砂糖，升級為大量肉桂糖粉、核桃碎、杏仁碎的混合裹料，並且發明了在空心的筒內填充冰淇淋、奶油、巧克力醬、草莓等各種餡料的吃法，變得極其華麗和上相。許多捷克本地人會指出，這種填充式的塔塔蜜並非捷克傳統食物，而是21世紀的「旅遊發明」。
